Most people do not fear “public speaking.” They fear **status risk**—looking foolish, blanking out, or being judged. That fear is ancient; the fix is modern and trainable: get out of your head, into structure, and into the other person.

## The real problem is not the stage

Fear of speaking has an evolutionary basis. In small ancestral groups, relative status meant access to resources. Anything that put status at risk—a crowd, a boss conversation, a high-stakes Q&A—still triggers that alarm.

Two consequences follow:

1. **Self-monitoring kills presence.** The more you score yourself mid-sentence, the less bandwidth you have to connect and adapt.
2. **Memorisation makes blanking more likely.** You create a “correct” script, then spend cognitive load comparing what you *meant* to say with what you *are* saying. That comparison is the trap.

**Rule:** Road map and structure, not rote. If you need exact wording or a number, put it on a card and read it. Better that than carrying the whole speech in working memory.

## Authenticity is not “winging it”

Authenticity is not the absence of preparation. It is:

1. Knowing what you stand for and where the value is **for you**
2. Converting that into something **useful for the audience**
3. Delivering without constant performance surveillance

Credibility has two flavours:

| Type | What it is | When to use it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Career / college** | Titles, LinkedIn, resume | Background, not the opener |
| **Costco** | Free sample—show value in the room | First: questions, relevance, engagement |

Start like an action movie: **action first**. Provocative statement, question, sharp statistic, or clear stake for the listener. Credentialing can wait. You demonstrate credibility; you do not announce it.

Success is not “I got my content out.” Success is **fidelity**—the audience can use what you said. Design for their needs, not your dump of knowledge.

## Structure beats lists

Brains are weak at remembering litany. They are strong at story-shaped packages. Useful structures:

- **Problem → solution → benefit** (classic ads; works in pitches)
- **What → so what → now what** (feedback, asks, experiment plans)
- **Past → present → future** (change narratives)

Bullet slides that are only lists often work against you. Prefer a logical spine the audience can ride.

**Tour-guide mindset:** Set expectations of the journey, signal transitions, allow short meanders, end with something they can take away (the “gift shop”). For strict education, stay mostly linear and scaffolded; for engagement, keep a linear spine and branch at points.

## Get out of your head: judgment drills

Abrahams uses a simple improv drill: for ~15 seconds, point at objects and name them **something they are not**. Harder than it sounds—because judgment floods in (“not wrong enough,” “not creative enough”).

What the drill reveals:

- We judge constantly while speaking
- We lock into **heuristics**—mental shortcuts that reduce uncertainty but can miss the moment

**Example heuristic failure:** After a bad meeting, someone asks “How do you think that went?” You assume they want a critique list. They may have wanted support. Listen before you lock the pattern.

Children are good at these games; adults need permission. Cloud-watching, random-object talks, and other low-stakes improvisation rebuild confidence that magic happens *in the moment*—and that you can prepare to be spontaneous the way athletes drill for the game.

## Practice like an athlete, not like a reader

Nobody thinks their way to better communication. The loop is:

1. **Repetition** — say it out loud; shadow practice is not the bout
2. **Reflection** — what worked / what did not
3. **Feedback** — trusted others who will be honest

A concrete habit Abrahams uses: nightly, write one or two things that went well and one or two that did not in that day’s communication. Weekly, review and plan one improvement. Years of that compounds.

Also record yourself. Watch three ways: audio only, video only, both. Uncomfortable; highly informative. Borrow styles from distant genres without copying—physicality, stillness, pacing—so you expand your range while staying yourself.

**Warm up before you speak.** Conversation before a keynote beats silence-to-brilliance. Athletes warm up; speakers often do not. Even mundane chat switches the brain into turn-taking mode and burns off freeze energy.

## Anxiety: treat symptoms and sources

### Body

- Anxiety raises heart rate, shortens breath, pushes the voice into the head
- Prefer **exhale-emphasised** breathing (longer, slightly stronger exhale). Physiological sighs (double inhale, long exhale) remain a fast calm-down
- Cool the palms (thermoregulators)—cold object in hand can reduce blushing and sweating
- Move with purpose: transitions and setup, **stillness on the punchline**; pacing that distracts hurts landing
- Stay on normal caffeine routine; do not spike caffeine because you slept poorly

### Mind

- Rationalise blanking: estimate likelihood (often ~20%); accept short embarrassment as survivable—this lowers the fear that causes blanking
- Orient to **what you want to happen** (value delivered), not the catastrophe movie
- Become present: conversation, walk, playlist, count backwards by 17s, or say a tongue twister out loud three times fast (present + voice warm-up)
- Build an **anxiety management plan** you can invoke; owning the plan reduces anxiety by itself

### If you blank

1. Retrace: repeat the last clear point
2. Soft reset: “I get passionate and get ahead of myself—give me a moment”
3. Buy time with a question to the room (“How would you apply this?”) while you recover
4. Do **not** pre-apologise for nerves—you prime the audience to hunt for weakness

Contingency planning (tech fails, spill, tough interrupt) builds the same calm you bring to travel delays.

## Spontaneous speaking is most of life

Planned decks matter. Most communication is still: unexpected questions, feedback in the corridor, fixing a mistake, small talk in the Uber.

**Lead with curiosity.** Ask; observe; comment on the environment. Conversation is turn-taking: supportive turns deepen the other person’s thread; switching turns introduce yours. Good talk does both.

Three words that draw reticent people out: **Tell me more.** After they answer, give space. Pre-work: know what might be valuable to them, then latch onto what they reveal.

Introducing yourself: lead with something you care about, then your name. You stand out, your voice has emotion, and accents become easier for others to parse.

When “going around the table,” listen hard enough to invent a question for each person—keeps you out of the anxiety ramp as your turn approaches. Prefer paired intros when you facilitate.

## Cadence, visuals, and audience expectation

Talks have song-like rhythm: openers, pace changes, repeats, pattern breaks. Monotone cadence loses people; deliberate disruption (a cold call, a short exercise, a shift of mode) re-engages.

Visuals: sparse beats dense. Too much detail on a slide or diagram steals hearing; pure stick figures under-inform. Aim for the anatomist’s sweet spot—enough structure to imprint, not a firehose. When the slide changes, eyes jump; do not put critical words only in that transition.

Younger audiences often need more mode switches in long sessions. Multi-generational workplaces mismatch expectations (fast/transactional vs slow/relational). Adjust channel and pace to the other person; front-loaded trust still pays.

Writing helps speaking—not instead of speaking, but as thought rehearsal: audience, scaffolding, word choice, then edit. Building your own slides (not Frankenstein decks) forces the story.

There is no single “correct” presence: introversion, quiet-but-not-shy, and neurodiversity can all communicate well. Play to strengths; expand range with low-stakes drills. One-on-one skill and stage skill overlap (structure, goal, strong open) but presence does not always transfer—train both.

## Damage control and hard conversations

- **Interruptions:** set boundaries up front (“five minutes of foundation, then questions”); reclaim the floor with a **paraphrase** (“Cost is critical—and that is next on the agenda”)
- **Filler words:** not evil; they hold the floor and sometimes signal importance. Reduce *distracting* ones—especially ums in silence—by **landing phrases**: finish out of breath so you must inhale (you cannot um while inhaling). Practice by reading your calendar aloud, landing each line
- **Ask for a raise / a decision:** check context (timing on their calendar); frame from *their* criteria and the value you created; practice out loud
- **Arguments:** listen until the phantom fight dissolves; declare how important the issue is on a scale so passion levels match before you escalate
- **Cross-culture / accents:** goal is message fidelity, not native-sounding English—repeat via story, example, analogy
- **Apologies and pre-loads:** do not open with “I’m jet-lagged / unprepared / sorry.” Start confident; apologise later only if needed

If humour or a high-risk hook (unusual opener, demo-as-art) is on the table: AB-test it. When it lands, it violates expectations in service of the goal; when it fails, it disconnects hard.

## A short prep checklist before your next high-stakes moment

1. **Audience** — What do they need relative to this topic? (Curse of knowledge and curse of passion are real.)
2. **Goal** — Know / feel / do: information, emotion, action
3. **Structure** — Pick one spine; cut clock-building; parachute into the story
4. **Practice out loud** — Anticipate responses; role-play hard turns
5. **Anxiety plan** — Breath, cool palms, tongue twister, warm-up conversation, movement rules
6. **Recovery plan** — Blank, interrupt, tech fail: know your next move
7. **After** — Reflect; seek selective feedback; iterate

## Key takeaways

| Principle | Practice |
| --- | --- |
| Status fear is normal | Reframe: you have value; they want value |
| Do not memorise | Structure + familiarity + note cards for precision |
| Connection over credentials | Hook first; Costco credibility |
| Get out of your head | Improv drills; heuristics awareness; presence |
| Athlete model | Reps, reflection, feedback; warm up |
| Manage anxiety deliberately | Symptoms + sources; written plan |
| Curiosity draws people out | Questions + “tell me more” |
| Fidelity over output | Design for what they can use |

Confidence is not the absence of nerves. It is a trained ability to stay present, structured, and useful—on stage, on a call, and in the conversation you did not see coming.
