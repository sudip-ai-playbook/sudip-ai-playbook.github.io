The EU AI Act is the Union’s binding, risk-based rulebook for placing AI systems and general-purpose AI models on the market, putting them into service, and using them. This article maps the regulation’s structure so teams can classify a use case, identify roles, and know which obligations and dates apply.

<ExecSummary>

## 1. What the Act is for

**Article 1** sets three linked aims:

1. Improve the functioning of the internal market
2. Promote human-centric, trustworthy AI
3. Protect health, safety, fundamental rights (including democracy, rule of law, and environmental protection) while supporting innovation

The Act therefore lays down:

| Pillar | What it covers |
| --- | --- |
| Harmonised rules | Placing on the market, putting into service, and use of AI systems |
| Prohibitions | Certain unacceptable AI practices |
| High-risk rules | Requirements for systems + obligations for operators |
| Transparency | Rules for certain AI systems (interaction, synthetic content, deepfakes) |
| GPAI | Rules for placing general-purpose AI models on the market |
| Oversight | Market monitoring, surveillance, governance, enforcement |
| Innovation support | Sandboxes and SME-focused measures |

## 2. Who is in scope (and who is out)

### 2.1 In scope — Article 2(1)

The Regulation applies to:

| Actor | Trigger |
| --- | --- |
| **Providers** | Place AI systems or GPAI models on the Union market / put AI systems into service — whether established in the EU or a third country |
| **Deployers** | Established or located in the Union |
| **Third-country providers/deployers** | Output of the AI system is used in the Union |
| **Importers and distributors** | AI systems in the supply chain |
| **Product manufacturers** | Place an AI system on the market with their product under their name/trademark |
| **Authorised representatives** | Of non-EU providers |
| **Affected persons** | Located in the Union |

### 2.2 Out of scope or limited — key carve-outs

| Exclusion | Essence |
| --- | --- |
| Military, defence, national security | Exclusive use for those purposes (with careful dual-use logic) |
| Scientific R&D only | Systems/models developed and put into service solely for scientific research and development |
| Pre-market research/testing | Activity before placing on the market / putting into service (real-world testing is **not** excluded) |
| Purely personal non-professional use | Deployer obligations do not apply to natural persons in that capacity |
| Open-source (limited) | Free/open-source AI systems generally out — **except** high-risk, Article 5, or Article 50 systems |
| International LE/judicial cooperation | Third-country public authorities / international organisations under cooperation frameworks with adequate safeguards |
| Data protection law | GDPR, LED, ePrivacy, etc. continue to apply; the Act does not replace them |

**Practical rule:** If the output is used in the Union, assume territorial reach even when the model runs outside the EU.

## 3. Core definitions you must get right

**Article 3** definitions drive every classification. The ones teams misuse most:

### 3.1 AI system (Art. 3(1))

A machine-based system that:

- Is designed to operate with **varying levels of autonomy**
- May exhibit **adaptiveness** after deployment
- For explicit or implicit objectives, **infers** from input how to generate outputs (predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions) that can influence physical or virtual environments

Inference (learning, reasoning, modelling) is the distinguishing feature versus purely hard-coded automation.

### 3.2 Roles along the value chain

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Provider** | Develops (or has developed) an AI system / GPAI model and places it on the market or puts the system into service under its own name/trademark |
| **Deployer** | Uses an AI system under its authority (not personal non-professional use) |
| **Importer** | Places a third-country provider’s system on the Union market |
| **Distributor** | Makes a system available in the supply chain (not provider/importer) |
| **Operator** | Provider, product manufacturer, deployer, authorised representative, importer, or distributor |
| **Downstream provider** | Integrates an AI model into an AI system |

**Substantial modification** or putting your own name/trademark on a system can shift who is the provider (Article 25).

### 3.3 Biometrics-related terms (high consequence)

| Term | Short meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Biometric identification | Establish identity by comparing biometric data to a database |
| Biometric verification | One-to-one authentication (“are you who you claim to be?”) — often treated differently from remote identification |
| Remote biometric identification | Identify without active involvement, typically at a distance, against a reference database |
| Real-time RBI | Capture, comparison, and identification without significant delay |
| Emotion recognition | Infer emotions/intentions from biometric data |
| Biometric categorisation | Assign persons to categories from biometric data |

## 4. The risk ladder (how the Act is organised)

```text
Unacceptable risk  →  Prohibited (Chapter II / Art. 5)
High risk          →  Mandatory requirements + operator duties (Chapter III)
Limited / transparency risk → Disclose / label (Chapter IV / Art. 50)
Minimal risk       →  No specific Act duties (codes of conduct encouraged)
GPAI models        →  Parallel regime (Chapter V), including systemic-risk layer
```

Supporting layers:

- **Chapter VI** — sandboxes, real-world testing, SME measures  
- **Chapters VII–IX** — AI Office, Board, EU database, market surveillance  
- **Chapter XII** — penalties

## 5. Prohibited AI practices (Article 5)

These apply from **2 February 2025**. Member States set penalties; the upper band for prohibited practices can reach **€35M or 7%** worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher) for undertakings.

### 5.1 The prohibition list (structured)

| Point | Practice | Core idea |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **(a)** | Subliminal / manipulative / deceptive techniques | Materially distort behaviour → significant harm |
| **(b)** | Exploitation of vulnerabilities | Age, disability, or specific social/economic situation → significant harm |
| **(c)** | Social scoring | Score over time from behaviour/traits → detrimental treatment in unrelated contexts or disproportionate treatment |
| **(d)** | Crime-risk prediction from profiling alone | Predict offending risk **solely** from profiling / personality traits (human assessment of objective facts can still be supported) |
| **(e)** | Untargeted facial scraping | Create/expand facial recognition DBs from internet/CCTV scraping |
| **(f)** | Emotion inference at work/education | Infer emotions in workplace or education — except medical/safety intent |
| **(g)** | Sensitive biometric categorisation | Infer race, political opinions, trade-union membership, religion/beliefs, sex life/sexual orientation from biometrics (labelling/filtering of lawful LE datasets excepted) |
| **(h)** | Real-time RBI in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement | **Banned by default**, with narrow, heavily conditioned exceptions |

### 5.2 Real-time RBI exceptions (Art. 5(1)(h)) — only if strictly necessary

1. Targeted search for victims of abduction, trafficking, sexual exploitation; search for missing persons  
2. Prevention of a specific, substantial, imminent threat to life/physical safety, or a genuine present/foreseeable terrorist threat  
3. Localisation/identification of a suspect of an Annex II offence punishable by ≥ 4 years custodial sentence in the Member State  

Even then: prior judicial/independent authorisation (urgency rules), fundamental-rights impact assessment, EU database registration, temporal/geographic/personal limits, and **no adverse legal decision based solely on the system output**. Member States may choose **not** to enable these exceptions at all.

## 6. High-risk AI systems (Chapter III)

### 6.1 Two classification routes — Article 6

**Route A — Product safety (Art. 6(1))**  
High-risk if the AI system is (or is a safety component of) a product under Annex I Union harmonisation legislation **and** that product requires third-party conformity assessment.

**Route B — Annex III use cases (Art. 6(2))**  
Stand-alone high-risk uses in the areas below, unless the Art. 6(3) derogation applies.

### 6.2 Annex III areas (map)

| # | Area | Examples of covered uses |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Biometrics | Remote biometric identification; sensitive biometric categorisation; emotion recognition (where permitted) |
| 2 | Critical infrastructure | Safety components in critical digital infrastructure, road traffic, water/gas/heating/electricity |
| 3 | Education & vocational training | Admission/assignment; evaluating learning outcomes; level-of-education decisions; exam proctoring |
| 4 | Employment & workers’ management | Recruitment/selection; decisions on terms, promotion, termination; task allocation; performance monitoring |
| 5 | Essential private/public services | Public benefits eligibility; credit scoring (fraud detection excepted); life/health insurance risk & pricing; emergency call triage/dispatch |
| 6 | Law enforcement | Victim-risk assessment; polygraphs; evidence reliability; certain reoffending risk tools; profiling in investigation (where permitted) |
| 7 | Migration, asylum, border control | Polygraphs; entry/irregular-migration/health risk; asylum/visa eligibility support; detection/recognition/identification (travel-doc verification excepted) |
| 8 | Justice & democratic processes | Assisting judicial authorities in applying law to facts; influencing election/referendum outcomes or voting behaviour (admin campaign logistics tools generally out) |

### 6.3 Non-high-risk derogation — Article 6(3)

An Annex III system is **not** high-risk if it does **not** pose a significant risk of harm to health, safety, or fundamental rights — including by not materially influencing decision outcomes — **and** one of these applies:

1. Narrow procedural task  
2. Improves a previously completed human activity  
3. Detects decision-pattern deviations without replacing the human assessment (with proper review)  
4. Preparatory task only  

**Hard stop:** If the system performs **profiling** of natural persons, it remains high-risk.  
Providers relying on the derogation must **document** the assessment and **register** the system (Art. 49(2)).

### 6.4 Mandatory requirements for high-risk systems — Section 2

| Article | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| **9** | Risk management system (lifecycle, iterative) |
| **10** | Data and data governance (quality, bias, representativeness) |
| **11** | Technical documentation (Annex IV) |
| **12** | Record-keeping / automatic logs |
| **13** | Transparency + instructions for use for deployers |
| **14** | Human oversight (incl. two-person rule for certain biometric ID uses) |
| **15** | Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity |

### 6.5 Provider obligations — Article 16 (headline list)

Providers must, among other duties:

- Ensure compliance with Section 2 requirements  
- Run a quality management system (Art. 17)  
- Keep documentation (Art. 18) and logs (Art. 19)  
- Complete conformity assessment (Art. 43) before market/service  
- Draw up EU declaration of conformity (Art. 47) and affix CE marking (Art. 48)  
- Register in the EU database where required (Art. 49)  
- Take corrective actions (Art. 20)  
- Meet accessibility requirements  

Non-EU providers appoint an **authorised representative** (Art. 22). Importers and distributors have verification and halt-if-noncompliant duties (Arts. 23–24).

### 6.6 Deployer obligations — Articles 26–27 (headline list)

Deployers must:

- Use systems per instructions for use  
- Assign competent human oversight  
- Ensure input data relevance/representativeness where they control inputs  
- Monitor operation; suspend and escalate if the system presents a risk  
- Keep logs under their control (≥ 6 months, subject to other law)  
- Inform workers before workplace deployment of high-risk AI  
- Inform natural persons subject to Annex III decisioning (with LED rules for LE)  
- Perform a **fundamental rights impact assessment** before certain public-sector / public-service / credit / insurance deployments (Art. 27)  
- Register use when they are public authorities (Art. 49(3))  

### 6.7 Conformity assessment — Article 43 (simplified)

| System type | Default path |
| --- | --- |
| Annex III point 1 (biometrics) | Internal control **or** notified-body assessment (Annex VII) — notified body required in several “no standards / partial application” cases |
| Annex III points 2–8 | Internal control (Annex VI) — Commission may later elevate some to Annex VII |
| Annex I product-related | Follow the product legislation’s procedure, with Chapter III Section 2 requirements integrated |

**Substantial modification** after placing on the market triggers a new assessment. Pre-determined learning changes documented at initial assessment are not substantial modifications.

## 7. Transparency obligations (Article 50)

Applies to certain systems **whether or not** they are high-risk.

| Who | Obligation |
| --- | --- |
| Providers of systems that interact directly with people | Design so people know they are interacting with AI — unless obvious to a reasonably informed person (LE detection tools: limited exception) |
| Providers of synthetic audio/image/video/text systems | Machine-readable marking that output is artificially generated/manipulated (assistive editing / non-substantial alteration excepted) |
| Deployers of emotion recognition / biometric categorisation | Inform exposed persons; process personal data under GDPR/LED as applicable |
| Deployers of deepfakes | Disclose artificial generation/manipulation (artistic/satirical works: lighter disclosure form) |
| Deployers of AI text informing the public on matters of public interest | Disclose AI generation — unless human review/editorial responsibility applies |

Information must be clear, distinguishable, timely, and accessible.

## 8. General-purpose AI models (Chapter V)

### 8.1 Concepts

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **GPAI model** | Model with significant generality, competent across a wide range of distinct tasks; can be integrated into many downstream systems (research/prototype-only before market excluded) |
| **GPAI system** | AI system based on a GPAI model that can serve many purposes |
| **Systemic risk** | Risk specific to high-impact capabilities with significant Union-market impact or serious negative effects on health, safety, security, rights, or society at scale |

### 8.2 Systemic-risk classification — Article 51

A GPAI model has systemic risk if:

1. It has **high-impact capabilities**, or  
2. The Commission designates it as equivalent (Annex XIII criteria)

**Presumption:** cumulative training compute **> 10²⁵ FLOP** → high-impact capabilities.

Providers must notify the AI Office within **two weeks** of meeting (or knowing they will meet) the threshold, with a narrow “specific characteristics” rebuttal path.

### 8.3 Obligations for all GPAI providers — Article 53

1. Technical documentation (Annex XI) — available to AI Office / national authorities  
2. Information for downstream providers (Annex XII)  
3. Copyright-compliance policy (incl. TDM opt-outs under Directive (EU) 2019/790 Art. 4(3))  
4. Publicly available summary of training content (AI Office template)

**Open-source relief:** free/open licence + public parameters/weights/architecture/usage info → (a)+(b) documentation duties eased — **not** for systemic-risk models, and **not** for copyright policy / training-content summary.

### 8.4 Extra obligations for systemic-risk GPAI — Article 55

- Model evaluation + adversarial testing  
- Systemic-risk assessment and mitigation at Union level  
- Serious-incident tracking and reporting  
- Adequate cybersecurity of model and physical infrastructure  

Codes of practice (Art. 56) are the intended compliance bridge until harmonised standards exist.

## 9. Innovation support (Chapter VI)

| Measure | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| **AI regulatory sandboxes** (Art. 57) | Controlled development/testing under supervision; at least one per Member State by 2 Aug 2026 |
| **Real-world testing** outside sandboxes (Art. 60) | Allowed for Annex III systems under plan, authority approval, consent, time limits, safeguards |
| **SME measures** (Art. 62) | Priority sandbox access, guidance channels, fee proportionality, templates |
| **Microenterprise QMS relief** (Art. 63) | Simplified quality-management elements (not a free pass on Arts. 9–15 duties) |

## 10. Governance and enforcement (map)

| Body / role | Function |
| --- | --- |
| **AI Office** (Commission) | Union expertise; GPAI supervision/enforcement; supports Board |
| **European Artificial Intelligence Board** | Member State coordination, advice, consistency |
| **Advisory forum** | Stakeholder technical input |
| **Scientific panel** | Independent experts; systemic-risk alerts |
| **National competent authorities** | Notifying authority + market surveillance authority; single point of contact |
| **EDPS** | Market surveillance for Union institutions/bodies |
| **EU database** (Art. 71) | Registration of Annex III high-risk systems (and Art. 6(3) “not high-risk” claims) |

Affected persons: complaint to market surveillance (Art. 85); right to explanation for certain Annex III deployer decisions (Art. 86); whistleblower protection via Directive (EU) 2019/1937 (Art. 87).

## 11. Penalties (Chapter XII) — order of magnitude

| Infringement class | Ceiling (undertakings: higher of amount or %) |
| --- | --- |
| Prohibited practices (Art. 5) | €35M or **7%** turnover |
| Most other operator/notified-body / Art. 50 breaches | €15M or **3%** turnover |
| Incorrect/incomplete/misleading information to authorities | €7.5M or **1%** turnover |
| GPAI provider breaches (Commission fines) | €15M or **3%** turnover |

For SMEs/start-ups, the **lower** of the percentage or fixed amount applies. Union institutions: EDPS fines (lower absolute caps).

## 12. Application timeline (Article 113)

| Date | What applies |
| --- | --- |
| **1 Aug 2024** | Entry into force (20 days after OJ publication; published 12 Jul 2024) |
| **2 Feb 2025** | Chapters I–II: general provisions + **prohibitions** |
| **2 May 2025** | GPAI codes of practice targeted ready |
| **2 Aug 2025** | Governance, notified bodies, GPAI obligations, penalties framework (excl. Art. 101 nuances), confidentiality (Art. 78) |
| **2 Aug 2026** | **General application** of the Regulation |
| **2 Aug 2027** | Art. 6(1) high-risk product rules + corresponding obligations; GPAI models already on market must have completed compliance steps (Art. 111(3)) |
| **2 Aug 2030 / 31 Dec 2030** | Extended transition for certain public-authority high-risk uses and large-scale FSJ IT systems (Art. 111) |

**Legacy systems:** High-risk systems on the market before 2 Aug 2026 generally need compliance only if subject to **significant design changes** thereafter — with stricter public-authority timelines.

## 13. Practitioner workflow (use this structure)

```text
1. Is it an AI system / GPAI model under Art. 3?
2. Is any Art. 2 exclusion engaged?
3. Which roles apply (provider / deployer / importer / distributor / manufacturer)?
4. Does Art. 5 prohibit the practice? → stop or redesign
5. High-risk?
   a. Annex I product safety component path?
   b. Annex III use case?
   c. Art. 6(3) derogation documented + registered?
6. If high-risk → Section 2 requirements + Art. 16/26 duties + conformity + CE + database
7. Art. 50 transparency applicable?
8. GPAI involved? → Art. 53 (+ 55 if systemic risk)
9. Evidence pack: risk management, data governance, docs, logs, oversight, FRIA/DPIA as needed
10. Calendar: which Art. 113 date binds this system?
```

Treat the Act as a **classification and evidence machine**, not a principles poster:

1. **Role** first  
2. **Prohibition** screen second  
3. **High-risk / transparency / GPAI** third  
4. **Dates and artefacts** last — then build
