# Business Communication

<ExecSummary>
A complete leadership communication and articulation programme: turn complex information into clear decisions, speak business language to executives and clients, listen with empathy, influence without authority, and practise deliberately with AI and human feedback.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- When preparing for Partner, Director, client or steering-committee conversations.
- When practising executive updates, difficult feedback, negotiation or crisis communication.
- Alongside [Business Power Words](./business-power-words) and [Communication and Executive Articulation](../28-communication-executive-articulation).
</WhenToUse>

## Purpose

This programme is designed to help a technical leader become someone who can:

- Turn complex information into clear decisions
- Communicate confidently with executives and clients
- Explain technical subjects in business language
- Listen with empathy and emotional intelligence
- Influence people without relying on formal authority
- Handle resistance, conflict and difficult questions
- Facilitate productive meetings
- Give feedback and develop team members
- Communicate effectively in writing and speaking
- Maintain composure in high-pressure situations
- Build trust, alignment and accountability

The aim is not simply to become a better speaker.

The aim is to become a leader who helps people:

- Understand the situation
- See why it matters
- Trust the direction
- Contribute meaningfully
- Make decisions
- Take action

## Part 1: The Complete Leadership Communication Model

Leadership communication can be organised into twelve capabilities.

### Capability 1: Clear thinking

The ability to understand a situation before communicating it.

You should be able to identify:

- The problem
- The relevant facts
- The assumptions
- The business impact
- The available options
- The trade-offs
- Your recommendation
- The risks
- The decision required
- The immediate next step

A communication problem is often a thinking problem.

When you do not understand your own position clearly, you may:

- Speak for too long
- Repeat yourself
- Add unnecessary detail
- Avoid making a recommendation
- Use technical language as protection
- Give information without direction

#### Clear-thinking exercise

Before an important conversation, complete:

- The current situation is...
- The real problem is...
- This matters because...
- The options are...
- The main trade-off is...
- My recommendation is...
- The evidence is...
- The risk is...
- The decision required is...
- The next step is...

### Capability 2: Articulation

Articulation is the ability to express an idea precisely and fluently.

It requires:

- Clear sentence construction
- Appropriate vocabulary
- Logical sequencing
- Concise explanations
- Confident delivery
- Strong transitions
- Relevant examples
- Controlled pace

Articulation is not about using sophisticated vocabulary.

A highly articulate leader can explain a difficult idea using simple language without losing accuracy.

#### Articulation principles

**One idea per sentence**

Instead of:

> We have reviewed the platform and there are several possible solutions, but there are security concerns and some integration challenges, and we may need additional time.

Say:

> We have completed the platform assessment. The preferred solution is technically viable. However, two security dependencies must be resolved before deployment. I recommend extending the pilot by two weeks.

**Use verbal signposting**

Examples:

- “There are three issues to consider.”
- “The first point concerns business value.”
- “The second concerns delivery risk.”
- “The final point is the decision we need today.”
- “Let me separate the immediate problem from the longer-term opportunity.”
- “The key distinction is...”
- “The implication for the client is...”
- “My recommendation is based on two factors.”

**Use precise uncertainty**

Do not pretend to know more than you do.

Say:

> We have high confidence in the architecture, moderate confidence in user adoption and low confidence in the current cost estimate.

This sounds stronger than:

> We are not really sure, but it should probably work.

### Capability 3: Audience adaptation

Different audiences need different information.

| Audience | Focus on |
| --- | --- |
| Executive | Strategic value, financial impact, competitive advantage, organisational risk, options and trade-offs, decisions required |
| CFO or finance | Investment, total cost of ownership, return on investment, unit economics, cost uncertainty, financial risk, benefits realisation |
| CTO or architecture | Technical feasibility, architecture, scalability, security, integration, reliability, operational support |
| Risk and compliance | Accountability, controls, evidence, traceability, legal obligations, residual risk, monitoring, incident management |
| Product or business leader | Customer problem, process improvement, user experience, adoption, operational impact, ownership, measurable outcomes |
| Delivery team | Purpose, scope, priorities, dependencies, responsibilities, deadlines, support |
| Team member | Expectations, development, feedback, recognition, psychological safety, support, ownership |

#### Audience adaptation exercise

Take one topic and explain it to:

- A CEO
- A CFO
- A CTO
- A risk director
- A project manager
- A junior engineer

The facts should remain consistent, but the emphasis should change.

### Capability 4: Vocal delivery

A strong message can lose impact if the delivery sounds rushed, uncertain or monotonous.

Train:

- Pace
- Volume
- Pausing
- Tone
- Emphasis
- Pronunciation
- Energy
- Breathing
- Vocal variety

**Pace**

Speak slowly enough to be understood.

A useful speaking pace for leadership communication is controlled and conversational. Avoid racing through important points.

**Pausing**

Pause:

- Before answering a difficult question
- After stating your recommendation
- Before presenting evidence
- Before delivering an important conclusion
- When interrupted
- When emotions are rising

Pausing helps you appear composed and gives the audience time to process.

**Emphasis**

Emphasise the words that carry meaning.

For example:

> The main risk is not the technology. It is user adoption.

**Filler-word reduction**

Common filler words include: um, like, basically, actually, you know, sort of, kind of, just, maybe, probably.

Do not try to remove every filler word immediately.

First:

1. Record yourself
2. Count recurring filler words
3. Select one filler word
4. Replace it with a pause

**AI voice-practice prompt**

> Listen to my spoken response and identify: where I spoke too quickly, where I needed to pause, words I overused, sentences that sounded uncertain, and places where vocal emphasis would improve meaning. Give me only three corrections, then ask me to repeat the answer.

### Capability 5: Non-verbal communication

Leadership communication includes how you appear while speaking.

Develop:

- Eye contact
- Facial expression
- Posture
- Hand gestures
- Physical stillness
- Camera positioning
- Attention
- Responsiveness

**In-person communication**

Aim to:

- Maintain natural eye contact
- Keep an open posture
- Avoid repetitive movements
- Use gestures to reinforce meaning
- Turn towards the person speaking
- Avoid checking devices
- Show attention through facial expression

**Virtual communication**

Check:

- Camera at eye level
- Face clearly visible
- Appropriate lighting
- Minimal background distractions
- Stable audio
- Looking into the camera during key points
- Avoiding constant self-view monitoring

#### Self-review exercise

Record a two-minute video and review it once without sound.

Ask:

- Do I look confident?
- Do I look engaged?
- Do my gestures support the message?
- Do I appear tense?
- Do I make distracting movements?
- Does my facial expression match the subject?

Then listen without watching.

Finally, review the complete video.

This separates visual and vocal problems.

### Capability 6: Listening and strategic questioning

Strong leaders do not simply speak well. They discover what needs to be said.

Listening has four levels.

| Level | Focus | Question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Facts | What happened? |
| 2 | Meaning | What does the person believe the situation means? |
| 3 | Emotion | What are they worried, frustrated or excited about? |
| 4 | Unstated issue | What are they avoiding saying directly? |

#### Types of leadership questions

| Type | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Clarifying | What do you mean by “the solution is not ready”? |
| Diagnostic | Which part of the process is creating the delay? |
| Evidence | What evidence supports that assumption? |
| Impact | What happens if we do not resolve this by Friday? |
| Perspective | How does the operations team see this issue? |
| Trade-off | Which is more important in this phase: speed or risk reduction? |
| Ownership | Who is accountable for making this decision? |
| Reflective | What would you do differently if you were starting again? |
| Commitment | What can you commit to completing by the next checkpoint? |

#### The five-question rule

In discovery, coaching or difficult conversations, ask at least five useful questions before presenting a solution.

This prevents premature problem-solving.

**AI listening practice**

> Act as a client with an unclear business problem. Reveal information gradually. Do not allow me to recommend a solution until I have asked enough questions to understand: the problem, the affected users, the business impact, the existing process, constraints, risks, success measures and decision ownership. At the end, assess the quality of my questions.

### Capability 7: Empathy and emotional intelligence

Empathy is understanding another person’s position, emotions and needs.

It does not require agreement.

Use:

**Listen → Reflect → Validate → Explore → Respond → Align**

| Step | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Listen | Allow the person to finish. |
| Reflect | It sounds as though the changing priorities have made it difficult for you to plan your work. |
| Validate | That concern is understandable. The scope has changed twice without an adjustment to the timeline. |
| Explore | Which change has had the greatest impact? |
| Respond | We need a stronger change-control process. |
| Align | Let us freeze the scope until Friday and review future changes together. |

#### Emotional self-management

When triggered, use:

**Pause → Label → Separate → Choose → Respond**

| Step | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Pause | Do not react immediately. |
| Label | Identify the emotion: “I am feeling defensive.” |
| Separate | Separate fact from interpretation. The fact is that my proposal was challenged. My interpretation is that they do not trust my judgement. |
| Choose | Decide the outcome you want: “I want to understand the risk they see and maintain a productive discussion.” |
| Respond | Can you explain which assumption concerns you most? |

#### Emotional trigger journal

| Situation | Emotion | Automatic response | Impact | Better response |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| My design is challenged | Defensive | Over-explain | Audience loses trust | Ask what risk they see |
| Deadline is missed | Frustrated | Focus on blame | Team becomes guarded | Diagnose dependency |
| Executive interrupts | Anxious | Rush answer | Message becomes unclear | Pause and answer directly |

### Capability 8: Storytelling

Facts create understanding. Stories create meaning and memory.

Leadership storytelling should be concise and purposeful.

Use:

**Context → Tension → Decision → Action → Result → Lesson**

Example:

> The customer-service team was handling thousands of repetitive requests every week. Response times were increasing and employees were spending less time on complex cases. We introduced a controlled AI assistant that handled routine enquiries and escalated uncertain cases to people. Within the pilot, handling time improved while human oversight was maintained. The important lesson was that workflow integration mattered more than model sophistication.

#### Types of leadership stories

- Customer story
- Transformation story
- Failure and learning story
- Vision story
- Values story
- Team-recognition story
- Risk story
- Innovation story
- Change story

#### Storytelling rules

- Use one central message
- Avoid unnecessary background
- Include a meaningful tension
- Explain the decision
- Show the outcome
- End with the lesson
- Do not exaggerate
- Protect confidential information

**AI storytelling exercise**

> Give me a leadership topic and ask me to tell a two-minute story using context, tension, decision, action, result and lesson. Identify unnecessary detail, missing tension and whether the final lesson is clear.

### Capability 9: Data storytelling

Leaders often need to turn data into decisions.

Use:

**Observation → Meaning → Cause → Implication → Recommendation**

| Step | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Observation | Customer-service handling time increased by 18%. |
| Meaning | The team is becoming less efficient as demand grows. |
| Cause | Most of the increase comes from repetitive identity and account queries. |
| Implication | Without process intervention, service levels will continue to decline. |
| Recommendation | Automate the high-volume repetitive queries while maintaining human escalation for complex cases. |

#### Avoid presenting data without interpretation

Weak:

> The chart shows an 18% increase, 12% decrease and a 7% variation.

Stronger:

> The main message is that demand is growing faster than team capacity. Most of the pressure comes from three repetitive enquiry types. Addressing those could remove approximately half of the avoidable workload.

#### Data presentation questions

Before presenting data, ask:

- What is the main message?
- Why does it matter?
- What caused it?
- What decision should it influence?
- What uncertainty exists?
- What should happen next?

### Capability 10: Influence and persuasion

Influence is not manipulation.

It is helping people evaluate a recommendation and commit to action.

Use:

**Stakeholder concern → Shared objective → Evidence → Trade-off → Recommendation → Commitment**

Example:

> I understand your concern about increasing the pilot budget. We both want to prove value before committing to production. The additional cost comes from security testing and evaluation, which are required to establish whether deployment is responsible. We could reduce the initial cost by narrowing the use-case scope rather than removing those controls. My recommendation is to preserve the controls and reduce the number of workflows in the pilot. Can we agree that approach today?

#### Principles of ethical persuasion

- Understand the stakeholder’s interests
- Use evidence honestly
- Make uncertainty visible
- Explain trade-offs
- Avoid artificial urgency
- Do not hide material risk
- Give stakeholders meaningful choice
- Seek durable agreement rather than temporary compliance

#### Influence without authority

When you cannot command action, build:

- Credibility
- Relationships
- Shared goals
- Evidence
- Reciprocity
- Coalition support
- Clear benefits
- Low-friction next steps

#### Stakeholder influence map

| Stakeholder | Interest | Influence | Main concern | Desired outcome | Communication approach |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| CFO | Cost and value | High | Uncertain return | Pilot approval | Quantify cost and benefits |
| Risk director | Compliance | High | Data exposure | Control agreement | Show safeguards and evidence |
| Product owner | Adoption | Medium | Workflow disruption | Operational ownership | Focus on user experience |
| Engineering lead | Feasibility | Medium | Integration complexity | Technical support | Clarify architecture and dependencies |

### Capability 11: Negotiation and conflict management

Conflict often comes from:

- Different objectives
- Competing priorities
- Unclear ownership
- Limited resources
- Different risk tolerances
- Poor information
- Past relationship problems
- Misunderstood intentions

#### Conflict-resolution framework

**Position → Interest → Evidence → Options → Trade-off → Agreement**

| Step | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Position | What is each person asking for? |
| Interest | Why do they want it? |
| Evidence | What facts matter? |
| Options | What alternatives exist? |
| Trade-off | What does each option gain or sacrifice? |
| Agreement | What will happen, who owns it and when? |

Example:

> You are requesting production deployment this month. I am recommending an extended pilot. It appears that your main concern is the commercial deadline, while mine is the unresolved safety threshold. Let us separate the launch date from the full production scope. We could launch with a smaller user group while completing the remaining evaluation. That protects the market commitment while reducing exposure.

#### De-escalation language

- “Let us slow this down and separate the issues.”
- “I think we agree on the objective but differ on the risk.”
- “Can we identify the assumption behind each position?”
- “What evidence would change your view?”
- “Let us focus on the decision rather than the person.”
- “I may be missing something. Help me understand your concern.”
- “We do not need complete agreement, but we do need a workable decision.”

**AI conflict practice**

> Act as a senior stakeholder who disagrees strongly with my recommendation. Make the disagreement realistic but professional. Evaluate whether I: remain calm, understand the underlying interest, avoid becoming defensive, use evidence, offer alternatives and establish a clear agreement.

### Capability 12: Leadership presence

Leadership presence is the combined effect of:

- Clarity
- Calmness
- Credibility
- Confidence
- Consistency
- Judgement
- Empathy
- Accountability

It does not mean being loud, dominant or always certain.

#### Behaviours that strengthen presence

- Leading with the main point
- Speaking at a controlled pace
- Holding pauses
- Making clear recommendations
- Admitting uncertainty precisely
- Listening without becoming passive
- Staying calm when challenged
- Taking accountability
- Giving credit to others
- Making decisions
- Following through

#### Behaviours that weaken presence

- Speaking without structure
- Over-explaining
- Apologising unnecessarily
- Avoiding recommendations
- Becoming defensive
- Blaming others
- Using excessive jargon
- Interrupting
- Changing position without explanation
- Failing to close discussions with action

## Part 2: Core Leadership Communication Frameworks

Do not use frameworks mechanically. Use them to organise thinking.

### Framework 1: Executive update

**Position → Impact → Recommendation → Risk → Decision**

> The programme is two weeks behind because identity integration remains unresolved. The delay affects the pilot start date but not the overall business case. I recommend moving the training and evaluation work forward while the identity issue is resolved. The principal risk is compressed testing time. We need agreement today on the revised sequencing.

### Framework 2: Recommendation

**Recommendation → Reasons → Evidence → Trade-off → Action**

> I recommend option B. It offers the best balance of implementation speed, security and operating cost. The main trade-off is reduced flexibility during the pilot. We need approval to begin detailed design.

### Framework 3: Technical explanation

**Problem → Concept → Mechanism → Value → Limitation → Example**

> The problem is that a general language model does not automatically know the company’s current policies. Retrieval-augmented generation solves this by finding relevant approved documents and providing them to the model before it responds. This improves accuracy and traceability. It does not eliminate hallucination, so evaluation and output controls remain necessary.

### Framework 4: Difficult feedback

**Situation → Behaviour → Impact → Expectation → Support**

> In Tuesday’s client meeting, the estimate was presented as confirmed before the assumptions were validated. This reduced confidence in the plan. In future, I need confirmed estimates and assumptions to be clearly separated. I will help review the next update before the meeting.

### Framework 5: Empathetic conversation

**Acknowledge → Clarify → Explore → Respond → Align**

> I understand that the repeated changes have been frustrating. Which change has affected your work most? What would help stabilise the plan? I agree that we need clearer scope control. Let us review all new requests together until the current sprint is complete.

### Framework 6: Objection handling

**Acknowledge → Answer → Evidence → Trade-off → Recommendation**

> I understand the concern about cost. The additional expense comes from the production security controls. Removing them would lower the initial cost but increase regulatory and operational exposure. My recommendation is to retain the controls and reduce the pilot scope.

### Framework 7: Crisis update

**Incident → Impact → Containment → Investigation → Recovery → Accountability**

> A data-processing error affected a limited number of pilot transactions. The process has been stopped and access has been restricted. We are determining the root cause and validating the affected records. No production systems are involved. The next update will confirm impact, corrective action and restart criteria.

### Framework 8: Meeting facilitation

**Purpose → Outcome → Agenda → Discussion → Decision → Owner → Deadline**

> The purpose of today’s meeting is to choose the use case for the pilot. By the end, we need one prioritised use case, an accountable business owner and agreement on the discovery plan.

## Part 3: Meeting Leadership and Facilitation

A strong leader does not only contribute to meetings. They shape the quality of the discussion.

### Before the meeting

Define:

- Why the meeting is required
- What outcome is needed
- Which decisions must be made
- Who needs to attend
- What preparation is required
- What information should be shared beforehand
- What could block the discussion

### Opening the meeting

State:

- Purpose
- Required outcome
- Agenda
- Time available
- Decision method

Example:

> The purpose of today’s session is to agree whether the finance-document use case should proceed to pilot. By the end, we need a decision, named owners and agreement on the first two weeks of work. We have 45 minutes. I suggest we confirm the business case, review risk and feasibility, and then make the decision.

### During the meeting

Practise:

- Summarising
- Redirecting
- Inviting quieter voices
- Managing dominant speakers
- Separating facts from opinions
- Identifying disagreement
- Testing assumptions
- Clarifying decisions
- Protecting time

#### Useful facilitation language

- We have heard two different positions. Let me summarise them.
- I would like to hear from people who have not spoken yet.
- Is that a confirmed fact or an assumption we need to validate?
- We are discussing implementation detail before agreeing the decision.
- What would need to be true for us to support this option?
- We have ten minutes remaining. Which issue must we resolve today?

### Closing the meeting

Confirm:

- Decisions
- Actions
- Owners
- Deadlines
- Dependencies
- Open questions
- Next checkpoint

Example:

> We have agreed to proceed with the pilot. Sarah owns business requirements, Ahmed owns security assessment and I will coordinate the solution design. The first checkpoint is Thursday. The only open issue is confirmation of data access.

## Part 4: Written Leadership Communication

Leaders must also communicate clearly through:

- Email
- Chat
- Proposals
- Decision papers
- Executive summaries
- Risk updates
- Meeting notes
- Status reports
- Presentations

### The BLUF method

BLUF means “Bottom Line Up Front.”

Put the main point first.

Weak:

> Over the past few weeks, the team has been reviewing the data, and there have been several discussions with security and operations...

Stronger:

> I recommend delaying the pilot by one week because the data-access approval remains unresolved.

Then provide:

- Reason
- Impact
- Options
- Risk
- Action

### Leadership email structure

| Section | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Opening | State the purpose. |
| Context | Provide only what the recipient needs. |
| Recommendation or request | Make the required action explicit. |
| Deadline | State when it is needed. |
| Supporting detail | Include concise evidence. |

Example:

> I recommend approving the revised pilot scope by Wednesday. The original scope depends on two data sources that are not yet cleared for use. Reducing the pilot to the approved data will preserve the launch date and allow evaluation to begin. Please confirm whether you support the revised scope.

### Writing-quality checklist

Before sending, ask:

- Is the purpose clear in the first two sentences?
- Is the requested action explicit?
- Is the message appropriate for the recipient?
- Is any detail unnecessary?
- Are risks presented accurately?
- Are dates and ownership clear?
- Could the tone be misinterpreted?
- Is there confidential information that should be removed?
- Does the message create clarity or additional discussion?

## Part 5: Cross-Cultural and Inclusive Communication

In global organisations, communication styles differ.

Differences may include:

- Direct versus indirect communication
- Hierarchical versus informal interaction
- Comfort with disagreement
- Use of silence
- Decision-making speed
- Written versus verbal preference
- Attitudes towards risk
- Use of humour
- Expectations around feedback

### Inclusive communication principles

- Avoid assuming silence means agreement
- Explain unfamiliar terminology
- Do not rely heavily on idioms
- Provide written summaries
- Invite input in multiple ways
- Avoid interrupting people who need more processing time
- Clarify decisions explicitly
- Pronounce names correctly
- Give credit accurately
- Avoid interpreting communication style as capability

### Inclusive meeting questions

- We have heard several views. Is there a perspective we are missing?
- Would anyone prefer to add comments after the meeting in writing?
- Before we decide, can each workstream confirm its main concern?

## Part 6: Crisis and High-Pressure Communication

During a crisis, people need:

- Accuracy
- Calmness
- Transparency
- Direction
- Accountability
- Regular updates

### Crisis principles

- State what is known
- State what is unknown
- Avoid speculation
- Explain immediate containment
- Define ownership
- Set the next update point
- Protect confidentiality
- Correct misinformation quickly
- Do not minimise the impact
- Do not assign blame before investigation

### Crisis update template

> At 10:15, we identified an issue affecting the pilot data-processing workflow. The workflow has been paused and access restricted. We are currently validating the scope and root cause. There is no evidence at this stage that production data is affected. The incident lead is Sarah. The next confirmed update will cover impact, corrective action and restart criteria.

## Part 7: The 16-Week Communication Development Roadmap

### Week 1: Clear thinking

**Focus:** Main point, problem definition, recommendation, decision clarity

**Daily practice:** Convert one work situation into a six-line executive summary; separate fact, assumption and opinion; state the decision required

**Success measure:** You can explain a situation in under one minute without losing the main point.

### Week 2: Structure

**Focus:** Logical sequencing, signposting, grouping ideas, clear openings and endings

**Daily practice:** Use Situation → Impact → Recommendation → Risk → Action

**Success measure:** Listeners can repeat your main message accurately.

### Week 3: Conciseness

**Focus:** Shorter sentences, reduced repetition, fewer filler words, answer-first communication

**Daily practice:** Deliver the same message in three minutes, ninety seconds, forty-five seconds, twenty seconds and one sentence

**Success measure:** You retain meaning while reducing length.

### Week 4: Vocal delivery

**Focus:** Pace, pausing, tone, emphasis, breathing, filler reduction

**Daily practice:** Record one two-minute response. Review pace, pauses, filler words, energy, clarity and emphasis

**Success measure:** You sound controlled and confident rather than rushed.

### Week 5: Non-verbal communication

**Focus:** Eye contact, posture, gestures, facial expression, camera presence

**Daily practice:** Record a video explanation and review it without audio

**Success measure:** Your physical delivery supports rather than distracts from your message.

### Week 6: Audience adaptation

**Focus:** Executive, commercial, technical, risk and team language

**Daily practice:** Explain one issue to three different stakeholders

**Success measure:** Each explanation focuses on the audience’s concerns.

### Week 7: Listening

**Focus:** Clarifying, summarising, reflecting, identifying unstated concerns

**Daily practice:** Complete one AI scenario where you cannot recommend a solution until you summarise the problem accurately

**Success measure:** The other person confirms that you understand their position.

### Week 8: Questioning and discovery

**Focus:** Diagnostic, evidence, impact, trade-off and ownership questions

**Daily practice:** Run one client-discovery simulation

**Success measure:** You uncover the business problem before discussing technology.

### Week 9: Empathy and emotional intelligence

**Focus:** Emotional awareness, validation, self-management, perspective-taking

**Daily practice:** Role-play one frustrated stakeholder conversation

**Success measure:** You acknowledge concerns without losing direction.

### Week 10: Storytelling

**Focus:** Context, tension, decision, result, lesson

**Daily practice:** Tell one two-minute business story

**Success measure:** The audience remembers the message and lesson.

### Week 11: Data storytelling

**Focus:** Insight, meaning, implication, recommendation

**Daily practice:** Choose one chart or metric and explain what decision it should influence

**Success measure:** You move beyond describing numbers to interpreting them.

### Week 12: Influence

**Focus:** Stakeholder concerns, shared objectives, evidence, trade-offs, commitment

**Daily practice:** Present one recommendation to a sceptical AI stakeholder

**Success measure:** You secure a specific commitment or identify the true barrier.

### Week 13: Negotiation and conflict

**Focus:** Interests, options, trade-offs, de-escalation, agreement

**Daily practice:** Run one disagreement scenario

**Success measure:** You remain calm and produce a workable next step.

### Week 14: Feedback and coaching

**Focus:** Specific feedback, behaviour and impact, development questions, accountability, support

**Daily practice:** Give one simulated feedback conversation using Situation → Behaviour → Impact → Expectation → Support

**Success measure:** The feedback is clear, fair and actionable.

### Week 15: Meeting facilitation

**Focus:** Purpose, outcomes, participation, time management, decisions, follow-up

**Daily practice:** Facilitate a simulated steering meeting

**Success measure:** The meeting produces decisions, owners and deadlines.

### Week 16: Integrated leadership communication

**Focus:** Executive update, empathy, influence, risk, conflict, decision-making

**Run simulations involving:**

- A delayed programme
- A budget challenge
- A client escalation
- A team-performance issue
- A security incident
- A disagreement between business and technology

**Success measure:** You maintain clarity, composure and empathy while moving the situation forward.

## Part 9: Daily Practice Routine

### Full 30-minute version

| Block | Duration | Activity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Preparation | 5 minutes | Choose scenario, audience, objective, skill and time limit |
| First attempt | 3 minutes | Respond without AI assistance |
| Feedback | 5 minutes | Ask AI for main strength, highest-leverage weakness, audience interpretation and specific correction |
| Targeted drill | 5 minutes | Practise only the weakness |
| Second attempt | 3 minutes | Repeat the full response |
| Challenge | 3 minutes | Handle one objection or interruption |
| Compression | 3 minutes | Deliver a 30-second version |
| Reflection | 3 minutes | Record what improved, what remains difficult and where to apply the lesson |

### Ten-minute minimum version

1. Choose one scenario
2. Give a one-minute response
3. Ask AI for one weakness
4. Repeat the response
5. Give a 20-second summary

## Part 10: AI Practice Modes

Use AI in different modes to avoid repetitive practice.

| Mode | Role |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Coach | AI evaluates your communication |
| 2. Stakeholder | AI plays a specific audience |
| 3. Interrupter | AI interrupts when you become unclear or too technical |
| 4. Challenger | AI raises objections |
| 5. Listener | AI summarises what it understood from your explanation — reveals whether your message was actually clear |
| 6. Simplifier | AI identifies jargon and complexity |

## Part 11: Master AI Communication Coach Prompt

Copy and use this prompt at the start of a practice session:

```text
You are my leadership communication, articulation and executive-presence coach.

I am developing as an AI Solution Engineering Manager. I need to communicate effectively with executives, clients, business leaders, technical teams, risk stakeholders and delivery teams.

Train me through realistic deliberate practice rather than lectures.

At the beginning of each session, define:
- The scenario
- My role
- The audience
- The objective
- The communication skill being tested
- The time limit

Ask me to respond without preparation.

After my response:
- Summarise what you understood from my message.
- Score me from 1 to 5 on: Main point, Structure, Conciseness, Audience relevance, Business value, Evidence, Risk judgement, Confidence, Empathy, Listening, Action orientation, Vocal delivery, Executive presence
- Identify the single highest-leverage weakness.
- Explain: the behaviour, how the audience may interpret it, and the specific correction.
- Give me one short drill targeting that weakness.
- Ask me to repeat the response immediately.
- Challenge me with one realistic objection.
- Ask me to provide a 30-second executive version.

Do not write the ideal answer until I have attempted the scenario twice.
Do not provide generic praise.
Make the feedback behavioural, specific and demanding.
Track recurring weaknesses during the conversation.
Increase the difficulty as my performance improves.

Vary scenarios across:
- Executive updates
- Client discovery
- Commercial recommendations
- Technical explanations
- Risk escalation
- Difficult feedback
- Team conflict
- Negotiation
- Crisis communication
- Steering committees
- Storytelling
- Data presentations
- Written communication
```

## Part 14: Real-World Transfer

AI practice only matters if it improves real behaviour.

### Before an important interaction

Prepare:

- What is my main point?
- Why does it matter to this audience?
- What evidence supports it?
- What is the likely objection?
- What emotion may be present?
- What decision or action do I need?
- What should my final sentence be?

### During the interaction

Remember:

- Lead with the conclusion
- Pause
- Listen
- Ask questions
- Acknowledge concerns
- Use simple language
- Clarify trade-offs
- Define action

### After the interaction

Review:

- What did the audience understand?
- Where did I lose clarity?
- What objection surprised me?
- Did I listen properly?
- Did I appear defensive?
- Was a decision made?
- Were ownership and deadlines clear?
- What will I change next time?

## Part 15: Human Feedback System

AI should support, not replace, human feedback.

Every two weeks, ask a trusted colleague:

- What is one communication behaviour I should continue?
- What is one behaviour I should change?
- In which situations am I least clear?
- Do I give enough context or too much detail?
- Do I listen before responding?
- How do I come across when challenged?

Every month, ask someone senior:

- What would make my communication appear more credible and leadership-ready?

Record the answers and test them during AI practice.

## Part 16: The Most Important Rules

1. Speak before asking AI for an ideal answer.
2. Practise realistic leadership situations.
3. Focus on one weakness at a time.
4. Repeat immediately after feedback.
5. Practise both speaking and listening.
6. Train verbal and non-verbal delivery.
7. Practise written communication.
8. Adapt messages to different audiences.
9. Increase difficulty gradually.
10. Use human feedback alongside AI.
11. Apply at least one lesson in real work every week.
12. Track recurring patterns.
13. Practise difficult situations, not only comfortable ones.
14. Do not confuse confidence with certainty.
15. Do not use empathy as a way to avoid accountability.
16. Do not finish a session after feedback; always perform the correction.
17. Do not memorise scripts; memorise structures.
18. Do not aim to sound impressive; aim to create understanding and action.

## Final Leadership Communication Standard

Before finishing any important message, check whether you have answered:

- What is happening?
- Why does it matter?
- What do I recommend?
- What evidence supports it?
- What are the risks and trade-offs?
- What does the audience care about?
- What decision or action is required?
- Who owns the next step?
- When will it happen?

Your communication is successful when people leave with:

- Greater clarity
- Greater trust
- A shared understanding
- A responsible decision
- Clear ownership
- Commitment to action
