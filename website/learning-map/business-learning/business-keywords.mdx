# Business Keywords

<ExecSummary>
A working glossary of economics, finance, strategy, commercial and operating-model terms so you can speak the language of buyers and CFOs without defaulting to vendor jargon.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>

## 1. Economics and Macroeconomics

GDP: Total value of goods and services produced within a country.

Real GDP: GDP adjusted for inflation.

Nominal GDP: GDP measured using current prices.

GDP per capita: GDP divided by population.

Economic growth: Increase in economic output over time.

Business cycle: Repeating periods of expansion, peak, contraction and recovery.

Recession: Significant decline in economic activity.

Depression: Severe and prolonged economic contraction.

Inflation: General increase in prices.

Deflation: General decrease in prices.

Disinflation: Inflation continues, but at a slower rate.

Stagflation: High inflation combined with weak growth and unemployment.

CPI: Consumer Price Index, measuring changes in consumer prices.

PPI: Producer Price Index, measuring changes in producer prices.

Interest rate: Cost of borrowing money.

Base rate: Benchmark interest rate set by a central bank.

Monetary policy: Central-bank decisions concerning interest rates and money supply.

Fiscal policy: Government decisions regarding taxation and public spending.

Quantitative easing: Central bank purchases financial assets to increase liquidity.

Quantitative tightening: Central bank reduces financial assets or liquidity.

Money supply: Total amount of money available in an economy.

Exchange rate: Price of one currency relative to another.

Currency appreciation: Increase in a currency’s value.

Currency depreciation: Decrease in a currency’s value.

Purchasing Power Parity: Comparison of currencies based on purchasing power.

Unemployment rate: Percentage of the labour force without employment.

Labour productivity: Output produced per worker or working hour.

Consumer confidence: Measure of consumers’ optimism about the economy.

Business confidence: Measure of businesses’ expectations about future conditions.

Economic indicator: Statistic used to assess economic performance.

Leading indicator: Indicator expected to change before the economy changes.

Lagging indicator: Indicator that changes after the economy has changed.

Elasticity of demand: Sensitivity of demand to changes in price or another factor.

Elasticity of supply: Sensitivity of supply to changes in price.

Substitute: Alternative product satisfying a similar need.

Complement: Product whose demand is related to another product.

Network effect: Product becomes more valuable as more people use it.

Economies of scale: Average costs fall as production increases.

Economies of scope: Cost advantages from producing multiple related products.

Diseconomies of scale: Costs increase because the organisation becomes too large or complex.

Market failure: Market does not allocate resources efficiently.

Externality: Cost or benefit imposed on third parties.

Information asymmetry: One party has more information than another.

Moral hazard: Party takes additional risk because another party bears the consequences.

Adverse selection: Higher-risk participants are more likely to enter a transaction.

## 2. Market Structures and Competition

Perfect competition: Many suppliers sell similar products with limited pricing power.

Monopoly: One supplier controls the market.

Oligopoly: Small number of powerful suppliers dominate the market.

Monopolistic competition: Many suppliers offer differentiated products.

Market concentration: Degree to which a market is controlled by a few firms.

Market share: Company sales as a percentage of total market sales.

Category leader: Company with the strongest position in a market category.

First-mover advantage: Benefit from entering a market before competitors.

Fast-follower strategy: Entering after pioneers while learning from their mistakes.

Switching cost: Cost or difficulty experienced when changing providers.

Barrier to entry: Factor preventing new competitors from entering.

Barrier to exit: Factor making it difficult to leave a market.

Substitution risk: Risk that customers replace the offering with another solution.

Commoditisation: Process by which products become difficult to differentiate.

Price war: Competitors repeatedly reduce prices to gain customers.

Predatory pricing: Pricing below sustainable levels to eliminate competitors.

Market cannibalisation: New offering reduces sales of an existing offering.

Co-opetition: Competitors cooperate in some areas while competing in others.

## 3. Corporate Finance and Capital Structure

Capital structure: Combination of debt and equity used to finance the company.

Debt financing: Borrowing money that must be repaid.

Equity financing: Raising money by selling ownership.

Leverage: Use of debt to finance the business.

Gearing ratio: Debt relative to equity or total capital.

Debt-to-equity ratio: Total debt divided by shareholders’ equity.

Interest coverage ratio: Ability to pay interest from operating earnings.

Debt service: Principal and interest payments on debt.

Debt covenant: Condition a borrower must follow.

Covenant breach: Failure to meet a lending condition.

Revolving credit facility: Flexible borrowing arrangement up to an agreed limit.

Term loan: Loan repaid over an agreed period.

Bridge financing: Temporary financing until longer-term funding is arranged.

Working-capital facility: Borrowing used for short-term operating requirements.

Overdraft: Bank facility allowing account balances to fall below zero.

Bond: Tradable debt issued by a company or government.

Coupon: Interest paid on a bond.

Maturity: Date when debt must be repaid.

Principal: Original amount borrowed.

Collateral: Asset pledged to secure debt.

Secured debt: Borrowing supported by collateral.

Unsecured debt: Borrowing not backed by specific collateral.

Credit rating: Assessment of a borrower’s creditworthiness.

Default: Failure to meet financial obligations.

Refinancing: Replacing existing debt with new debt.

Recapitalisation: Changing the company’s debt-equity structure.

Dividend: Distribution of profits to shareholders.

Dividend yield: Annual dividend divided by share price.

Share buyback: Company repurchases its own shares.

Retained earnings: Profits kept within the business.

Cost of capital: Required return expected by lenders and investors.

Hurdle rate: Minimum return required before approving an investment.

## 4. Treasury and Cash Management

Treasury: Function responsible for liquidity, funding and financial risk.

Liquidity: Ability to meet short-term obligations.

Cash position: Amount of available cash at a particular time.

Cash concentration: Combining cash from multiple accounts.

Cash pooling: Group arrangement that manages multiple entities’ cash together.

Cash conversion cycle: Time between paying suppliers and receiving customer cash.

Days Sales Outstanding: Average time customers take to pay.

Days Payable Outstanding: Average time the company takes to pay suppliers.

Days Inventory Outstanding: Average time inventory remains unsold.

Liquidity buffer: Cash reserve maintained for uncertainty.

Restricted cash: Cash that cannot be freely used.

Idle cash: Cash not currently required for operations.

Treasury policy: Rules governing borrowing, investment and financial risk.

Foreign-exchange risk: Risk caused by currency movements.

Transaction exposure: Currency risk related to specific transactions.

Translation exposure: Accounting impact of converting foreign subsidiaries’ results.

Natural hedge: Matching income and expenditure in the same currency.

Forward contract: Agreement fixing an exchange rate for a future transaction.

Option: Right, but not obligation, to transact at a specified price.

Swap: Agreement to exchange financial cash flows.

Hedging: Reducing exposure to financial risks.

Counterparty risk: Risk that another party fails to meet obligations.

Bank mandate: Rules identifying authorised bank-account signatories.

## 5. FP&A and Management Accounting

FP&A: Financial Planning and Analysis.

Annual operating plan: Detailed one-year financial and operational plan.

Long-range plan: Financial plan covering several years.

Top-down planning: Senior management sets targets for the organisation.

Bottom-up planning: Teams build forecasts from detailed assumptions.

Driver-based planning: Forecasting based on operational drivers such as users or headcount.

Zero-based budgeting: Every cost must be justified from zero.

Incremental budgeting: Prior-year budget adjusted for expected changes.

Flexible budget: Budget that changes according to activity levels.

Scenario analysis: Comparing financial outcomes under different assumptions.

Sensitivity analysis: Testing how one variable affects the result.

Best case: Optimistic projected outcome.

Base case: Most likely projected outcome.

Worst case: Pessimistic projected outcome.

Actuals: Recorded financial results.

Latest estimate: Most recent expected financial outcome.

Budget variance: Difference between budget and actual results.

Price variance: Impact caused by paying or charging a different price.

Volume variance: Impact caused by selling or producing a different quantity.

Mix variance: Impact caused by changes in product or customer mix.

Favourable variance: Difference improving financial performance.

Adverse variance: Difference reducing financial performance.

Cost allocation: Assigning shared costs to departments or products.

Cost driver: Activity causing a cost to increase or decrease.

Fixed cost: Cost that does not change significantly with activity.

Variable cost: Cost that changes with output.

Semi-variable cost: Cost containing fixed and variable components.

Direct cost: Cost directly attributable to a product or project.

Indirect cost: Shared cost not directly attributable to one product.

Overhead: General operating cost.

Absorption costing: Allocating direct and indirect production costs to products.

Activity-based costing: Assigning costs based on activities that consume resources.

Standard costing: Comparing actual costs with predetermined expected costs.

Transfer pricing: Pricing transactions between related entities.

## 6. Financial Ratios

Current ratio: Current assets divided by current liabilities.

Quick ratio: Liquid current assets divided by current liabilities.

Gross margin: Gross profit divided by revenue.

Operating margin: Operating profit divided by revenue.

Net margin: Net profit divided by revenue.

Return on Assets: Profit generated relative to total assets.

Return on Equity: Profit generated relative to shareholder equity.

Asset turnover: Revenue divided by average assets.

Inventory turnover: Cost of sales divided by average inventory.

Receivables turnover: Revenue divided by average receivables.

Interest coverage: Operating earnings divided by interest expense.

Debt-service coverage ratio: Cash available for debt payments divided by required payments.

Earnings per share: Profit attributable to each ordinary share.

Price-to-earnings ratio: Share price divided by earnings per share.

Enterprise value: Equity value plus net debt and other adjustments.

EV-to-EBITDA: Enterprise value divided by EBITDA.

## 7. Revenue Recognition and Commercial Accounting

Revenue recognition: Rules determining when revenue appears in financial statements.

Performance obligation: Distinct promised good or service in a contract.

Contract asset: Earned revenue not yet unconditionally billable.

Contract liability: Payment received before fulfilling obligations.

Deferred revenue: Revenue invoiced or collected before service delivery.

Unbilled revenue: Revenue earned but not yet invoiced.

Recognised revenue: Revenue recorded during the accounting period.

Recurring revenue: Revenue expected to repeat.

Non-recurring revenue: One-time revenue.

Professional-services revenue: Revenue from implementation, consulting or support services.

Usage revenue: Revenue based on customer consumption.

Minimum commitment: Contractual minimum amount the customer must purchase.

Take-or-pay: Customer pays a minimum amount whether or not it uses the service.

Revenue backlog: Contracted revenue expected to be recognised later.

Remaining Performance Obligations: Contracted revenue not yet recognised.

Bad debt: Receivable unlikely to be collected.

Credit note: Document reducing the amount a customer owes.

Write-off: Removal of an unrecoverable amount from accounts.

## 8. Taxation

Corporation tax: Tax on company profits.

Income tax: Tax on individual income.

Capital gains tax: Tax on gains from selling assets.

VAT: Value Added Tax applied to goods and services.

Input VAT: VAT paid by the business on purchases.

Output VAT: VAT charged to customers.

VAT registration threshold: Revenue level at which VAT registration becomes required.

Payroll tax: Taxes connected with employee compensation.

Withholding tax: Tax deducted before payment to a recipient.

Tax residency: Country in which a person or entity is treated as resident for tax.

Permanent establishment: Sufficient business presence creating taxable obligations.

Tax deduction: Expense reducing taxable income.

Tax credit: Amount directly reducing tax payable.

Tax allowance: Amount or investment qualifying for favourable tax treatment.

Tax relief: Reduction in tax available under specific rules.

Tax loss carryforward: Using current losses against future taxable profits.

Deferred tax: Future tax consequences of temporary accounting differences.

Effective tax rate: Total tax expense divided by accounting profit.

Tax planning: Structuring activities efficiently within the law.

Tax avoidance: Legally arranging affairs to reduce tax, although some schemes may be challenged.

Tax evasion: Illegal concealment or misrepresentation to avoid tax.

R&D tax credit: Tax incentive for qualifying research and development expenditure.

Arm’s-length principle: Related-party transactions must reflect market conditions.

Double-taxation treaty: Agreement preventing the same income being taxed twice.

## 9. Legal Entities and Corporate Administration

Sole trader: Individual operating a business personally.

Partnership: Business owned by two or more partners.

Limited company: Separate legal entity with limited shareholder liability.

Limited liability partnership: Partnership structure with limited liability.

Public limited company: Company whose shares may be publicly offered.

Parent company: Company controlling another company.

Subsidiary: Company controlled by another company.

Holding company: Entity primarily created to hold investments or subsidiaries.

Group company: Entity within a broader corporate group.

Joint venture: Business arrangement jointly controlled by multiple parties.

Special Purpose Vehicle: Separate entity created for a specific project or transaction.

Registered office: Official legal address of a company.

Articles of association: Internal rules governing a company.

Shareholder agreement: Contract governing relationships among shareholders.

Certificate of incorporation: Document confirming legal formation.

Company secretary: Person responsible for corporate-administration obligations.

Ultimate Beneficial Owner: Individual who ultimately owns or controls an entity.

Persons with Significant Control: People with significant ownership or influence.

Statutory register: Legally required corporate record.

Annual accounts: Financial statements filed for a financial year.

Confirmation statement: Filing confirming company information remains accurate.

Solvency: Ability to meet long-term financial obligations.

Insolvency: Inability to pay debts or liabilities exceeding assets.

Administration: Formal process designed to rescue or restructure an insolvent company.

Liquidation: Process of closing a company and distributing its assets.

## 10. Share Capital and Ownership

Ordinary share: Standard ownership share carrying voting and economic rights.

Preference share: Share with preferential dividend or liquidation rights.

Voting rights: Shareholder rights to vote on company matters.

Share class: Category of shares with defined rights.

Authorised share capital: Maximum share capital permitted under older or applicable frameworks.

Issued share capital: Shares formally issued to shareholders.

Fully diluted ownership: Ownership assuming all options and convertible instruments convert.

Share premium: Amount paid above a share’s nominal value.

Option: Right to purchase shares at an agreed price.

Exercise price: Price at which an option may be used.

Restricted stock unit: Promise to provide shares after conditions are met.

Employee stock option plan: Scheme providing equity incentives to employees.

Drag-along right: Majority shareholders can require minority shareholders to participate in a sale.

Tag-along right: Minority shareholders can join a sale by majority shareholders.

Pre-emption right: Existing shareholders receive priority when new shares are issued.

Anti-dilution protection: Protection against dilution from lower-priced future fundraising.

Vesting schedule: Timeline over which equity becomes earned.

Good leaver: Employee leaving under circumstances allowing favourable equity treatment.

Bad leaver: Employee leaving under circumstances causing less favourable treatment.

## 11. Corporate Development and M&A

Corporate development: Internal function managing acquisitions, investments and strategic partnerships.

Deal thesis: Strategic reason for pursuing a transaction.

Target screening: Identifying potential acquisition candidates.

Indicative offer: Non-binding preliminary acquisition proposal.

Letter of intent: Document summarising proposed transaction terms.

Heads of terms: Outline of principal commercial terms.

Exclusivity period: Period during which the seller cannot negotiate with others.

Financial due diligence: Examination of financial performance and quality of earnings.

Commercial due diligence: Analysis of market, customers and competitive position.

Legal due diligence: Review of contracts, disputes and legal obligations.

Technical due diligence: Assessment of technology, architecture and technical risks.

Operational due diligence: Review of processes, people and delivery capabilities.

Cybersecurity due diligence: Assessment of security risks and controls.

Quality of earnings: Assessment of how sustainable and reliable profits are.

Net debt: Borrowings minus cash.

Working-capital adjustment: Purchase-price adjustment based on working capital at completion.

Locked-box mechanism: Purchase price fixed using a historical balance sheet.

Completion accounts: Final accounts used to adjust the purchase price.

Purchase-price allocation: Allocation of acquisition value across assets and goodwill.

Goodwill: Premium paid beyond the fair value of identifiable net assets.

Integration: Combining acquired operations with the buyer.

Integration Management Office: Team coordinating post-merger integration.

Day-one readiness: Ensuring critical operations function immediately after completion.

Synergy capture: Realising expected cost or revenue benefits.

Dis-synergy: Negative consequence arising from a transaction.

Stranded cost: Cost remaining after part of a business is sold.

Transition Services Agreement: Temporary services supplied after a sale.

## 12. Advanced Procurement and Strategic Sourcing

### Procurement structures

Procurement intake: Formal entry point for a purchasing request.

Intake-to-procure: Process from initial demand through supplier selection.

Strategic sourcing: Structured process for selecting suppliers and optimising value.

Operational procurement: Day-to-day ordering and invoice management.

Category strategy: Plan for managing a category of expenditure.

Spend category: Grouping of related goods or services.

Spend cube: Analysis of spending by supplier, category and business unit.

Demand management: Controlling and optimising what the organisation purchases.

Specification: Detailed description of the required product or service.

Statement of requirements: Formal description of business and technical needs.

Market sounding: Informal engagement with suppliers before formal tendering.

Supplier longlist: Broad list of potential suppliers.

Supplier shortlist: Reduced list invited to proceed.

Pre-qualification questionnaire: Initial supplier suitability assessment.

Invitation to tender: Formal request for competitive bids.

Expression of interest: Supplier indication that it wants to participate.

Request for solution: Procurement focused on outcomes rather than prescribed specifications.

Competitive dialogue: Procurement process involving structured dialogue with bidders.

Reverse auction: Suppliers compete by lowering prices electronically.

E-auction: Online competitive bidding process.

### Bid evaluation

Evaluation criteria: Factors used to assess bids.

Weighted scoring: Different evaluation criteria receive different importance.

Technical score: Assessment of capability and solution quality.

Commercial score: Assessment of price and commercial terms.

Price-quality ratio: Balance between cost and non-price value.

Commercial normalisation: Adjusting proposals so prices can be compared fairly.

Bid clarification: Questions asked to resolve unclear proposal content.

Bidder presentation: Supplier presentation explaining its proposal.

Reference check: Contacting previous customers to confirm supplier performance.

Proof of value: Demonstration of measurable business benefit.

Best and final offer: Supplier’s final improved submission.

Bid/no-bid decision: Supplier decision about whether to compete.

Award recommendation: Procurement’s documented preferred-supplier decision.

Standstill period: Period allowing unsuccessful bidders to challenge an award.

### Supplier economics

Total Cost of Ownership: Complete lifetime cost of buying and operating something.

Total Economic Impact: Wider financial impact, including benefits and avoided costs.

Landed cost: Product cost plus transport, tax, customs and related costs.

Should-cost model: Independent estimate of a reasonable supplier cost.

Open-book pricing: Supplier discloses its underlying cost structure.

Cost breakdown: Division of price into labour, materials, overhead and profit.

Rate card: Agreed prices for specified roles or services.

Blended rate: Combined average rate across different resource types.

Indexation: Contract price changes linked to inflation or another index.

Benchmarking clause: Right to compare pricing or performance with the market.

Most-favoured-customer clause: Customer receives pricing at least as favourable as comparable customers.

Gainshare: Supplier receives part of achieved savings or benefits.

Painshare: Supplier absorbs part of losses or cost overruns.

Target-cost contract: Parties agree a target and share differences.

### Supplier relationship management

Supplier segmentation: Classifying suppliers by value, risk or strategic importance.

Kraljic Matrix: Segments purchases into strategic, leverage, bottleneck and routine categories.

Strategic supplier: Supplier critical to competitive advantage or operations.

Leverage supplier: Supplier operating in a competitive market where the buyer has power.

Bottleneck supplier: Supplier associated with high supply risk but relatively low spend.

Routine supplier: Low-risk, low-value supplier.

Supplier Relationship Management: Structured management of important suppliers.

Supplier scorecard: Periodic evaluation of supplier performance.

Supplier QBR: Quarterly Business Review with a supplier.

Corrective action plan: Agreed steps to resolve supplier underperformance.

Supplier development: Helping suppliers improve their capability.

Supplier innovation: Using supplier knowledge to develop better solutions.

Dual sourcing: Using two suppliers for resilience or competition.

Multi-sourcing: Using several suppliers.

Supplier rationalisation: Reducing the number of suppliers.

Supplier diversification: Reducing dependency by adding alternative suppliers.

Fourth-party risk: Risk created by suppliers used by your direct suppliers.

### Purchase-to-pay

Purchase requisition: Internal request to purchase something.

Purchase order: Authorised commitment to buy.

Goods receipt note: Confirmation that goods or services were received.

Two-way match: Comparison of purchase order and invoice.

Three-way match: Comparison of purchase order, receipt and invoice.

Invoice exception: Invoice that cannot be automatically processed.

Vendor master data: Authoritative supplier information.

Duplicate invoice: Invoice entered more than once.

Payment terms: Agreed period before payment is due.

Net 30/60/90: Payment due 30, 60 or 90 days after an agreed event.

Early-payment discount: Discount for paying before the due date.

Dynamic discounting: Discount varies according to how early payment occurs.

Procurement card: Company card used for lower-value purchases.

No-PO-no-pay: Supplier invoice is rejected without an authorised purchase order.

### Procurement contract protection

Performance bond: Financial guarantee of supplier performance.

Parent-company guarantee: Parent organisation guarantees a subsidiary’s obligations.

Source-code escrow: Source code held by a third party for specified emergencies.

Step-in rights: Customer may temporarily take control of services after serious failure.

Transition assistance: Supplier support during transfer to another provider.

Exit management plan: Plan for ending the supplier relationship safely.

Subcontractor: Third party used by the primary supplier.

Flow-down obligations: Contractual duties passed to subcontractors.

Novation: Transfer of a contract to a new legal party.

Change order: Formal contract amendment.

Variation: Agreed change to scope, price or terms.

Service acceptance: Formal customer confirmation that services meet requirements.

Remediation period: Time given to correct contractual failure.

Material breach: Serious contractual violation.

Modern slavery due diligence: Supplier checks addressing forced labour risk.

Supplier code of conduct: Standards suppliers are expected to follow.

Conflict-minerals compliance: Controls relating to minerals linked to armed conflict.

Supplier diversity: Inclusion of underrepresented or smaller supplier groups.

## 13. Sales Operations and Revenue Operations

Revenue Operations: Integrated management of marketing, sales and customer-success operations.

Sales Operations: Function improving sales processes, tools and reporting.

Marketing Operations: Function managing marketing systems, data and campaigns.

Customer Success Operations: Function supporting customer-success processes and analytics.

Lead routing: Assigning leads to appropriate sales representatives.

Territory planning: Allocating accounts or regions to sales teams.

Account ownership: Responsibility for managing a customer account.

Named account: Specifically assigned strategic customer.

Whitespace analysis: Identifying products or business units not yet sold into an account.

Account penetration: Extent of relationships and revenue within a customer.

Pipeline generation: Creation of new qualified opportunities.

Pipeline velocity: Speed at which opportunities move and generate revenue.

Deal desk: Cross-functional team reviewing pricing, risk and contract terms.

Quote-to-cash: Process from preparing a quote through collecting payment.

Configure, Price, Quote: System for creating accurate commercial proposals.

Discount authority: Approval level required for a discount.

Deal slippage: Opportunity expected to close in one period but moves to another.

Forecast category: Classification such as pipeline, upside, best case or commit.

Sales forecast: Prediction of future closed revenue.

Commit: Deals sales leadership strongly expects to close.

Upside: Deals that may close but are less certain.

Sales productivity: Revenue or output generated per salesperson.

Ramp time: Time required for a salesperson to reach expected productivity.

Quota attainment: Percentage of sales target achieved.

Sales compensation: Pay structure for salespeople.

Commission: Variable payment linked to sales results.

Accelerator: Higher commission rate after exceeding target.

Clawback: Recovery of commission after cancellation or non-payment.

## 14. Enterprise Sales and Buying Committees

Buying committee: Group involved in an enterprise purchase.

Decision-maker: Person authorised to make or approve a decision.

Budget holder: Person controlling the relevant budget.

Technical buyer: Stakeholder evaluating technical suitability.

Security reviewer: Stakeholder assessing cybersecurity.

Procurement buyer: Stakeholder managing sourcing and commercial negotiation.

Legal reviewer: Stakeholder negotiating contractual terms.

End user: Person who will use the solution.

Blocker: Stakeholder or issue preventing progress.

Mobiliser: Stakeholder capable of driving internal change.

Champion: Internal advocate actively supporting the solution.

Executive sponsor: Senior stakeholder backing the initiative.

Compelling event: Deadline or event creating urgency.

Business pain: Problem with measurable organisational impact.

Decision criteria: Standards used to compare alternatives.

Decision process: Steps the customer follows before purchasing.

Paper process: Contract, procurement and approval steps after commercial agreement.

Mutual action plan: Shared plan of steps needed to close and implement a deal.

Procurement timeline: Time required for formal purchasing activities.

Sales objection: Concern preventing purchase.

Objection handling: Process of resolving customer concerns.

Competitive displacement: Replacing an incumbent provider.

Incumbent supplier: Existing provider.

Greenfield opportunity: Opportunity without an established solution.

Brownfield environment: Existing systems that must be integrated or replaced.

## 15. Customer Success and Account Management

Customer success: Proactive function helping customers achieve desired outcomes.

Account management: Commercial management of ongoing customer relationships.

Customer onboarding: Process of introducing and activating a new customer.

Implementation: Configuration and deployment of the solution.

Adoption plan: Plan for increasing customer usage.

Success plan: Document connecting customer objectives with activities and metrics.

Customer health score: Indicator of adoption, satisfaction and commercial risk.

Customer maturity: Degree of capability and sophistication in using a solution.

Executive Business Review: Strategic meeting with senior customer stakeholders.

Business review: Periodic assessment of performance, outcomes and priorities.

Renewal: Continuation of a customer contract.

Renewal rate: Percentage of contracts renewed.

Renewal risk: Likelihood that a customer will not renew.

Churn reason: Recorded reason for customer cancellation.

Contraction: Reduction in customer spending.

Expansion: Increase in existing customer spending.

Upsell: Sale of a higher-level offering.

Cross-sell: Sale of a different related offering.

Customer advocacy: Customers publicly recommending the company.

Reference customer: Customer willing to speak to prospects.

Case study: Documented customer problem, solution and result.

Voice of the Customer: Structured collection of customer feedback.

Customer effort score: Ease of completing an interaction.

Net Promoter Score: Likelihood that customers will recommend the company.

Customer satisfaction score: Satisfaction with a product or interaction.

Time to resolution: Time required to solve a customer issue.

First-contact resolution: Issues solved during the first interaction.

Escalation: Raising an issue to a higher support or management level.

Service recovery: Actions used to restore trust following failure.

## 16. Customer Support and Service Management

Support ticket: Recorded customer issue or request.

Ticket backlog: Unresolved support tickets.

Ticket deflection: Resolving issues through self-service before a ticket is created.

Knowledge base: Collection of support information and instructions.

Self-service: Customer resolves issues without direct staff assistance.

Service desk: Central point for user support.

Incident: Unplanned interruption or reduction in service quality.

Major incident: Severe incident requiring urgent coordinated response.

Problem: Underlying cause of one or more incidents.

Known error: Identified problem with a documented workaround.

Workaround: Temporary way of reducing an issue’s impact.

Service request: Standard request such as access or information.

Change: Addition, modification or removal affecting a service.

Change Advisory Board: Group reviewing significant changes.

Emergency change: Urgent change required to resolve a critical issue.

Release management: Planning and controlling software releases.

Configuration item: Component managed within a service environment.

Configuration Management Database: Record of service components and relationships.

Mean Time to Detect: Average time required to discover an incident.

Mean Time to Acknowledge: Average time required to begin responding.

Mean Time to Repair: Average time required to restore service.

Mean Time Between Failures: Average operating time between failures.

Post-incident review: Structured examination after an incident.

Root-cause analysis: Investigation of the underlying cause.

Error budget: Acceptable amount of service unreliability.

## 17. Product Portfolio and Product Economics

Product portfolio: Complete group of products managed by a company.

Product line: Related group of products.

Platform strategy: Building reusable capabilities supporting multiple products.

Product suite: Collection of integrated offerings.

Modular architecture: Product design using interchangeable components.

Product lifecycle: Introduction, growth, maturity and decline.

Product sunset: Planned retirement of a product.

End of life: Date after which a product is no longer supported.

Deprecation: Gradual withdrawal of a feature or service.

Feature parity: Different versions provide equivalent functionality.

Product cannibalisation: New product reduces demand for another company product.

Attach rate: Percentage of core-product sales including an additional product.

Feature adoption: Percentage of eligible users using a feature.

Product-qualified lead: User whose product behaviour indicates buying intent.

Product-led growth: Product usage drives acquisition and expansion.

Sales-led growth: Salespeople drive customer acquisition.

Community-led growth: Community participation drives adoption.

Product-market fit: Strong alignment between product and market demand.

Problem-solution fit: Evidence that the solution addresses a meaningful problem.

Founder-market fit: Founder expertise and credibility within the target market.

Product economics: Revenue, cost and profitability of a product.

Cost per inference: Cost incurred for each AI model request.

Gross-margin profile: Expected gross margin characteristics of an offering.

## 18. Pricing Strategy

Price architecture: Overall structure of plans, units and charges.

Pricing metric: Unit used to determine charges.

Value metric: Unit most closely linked to customer value.

Packaging: Grouping of features into commercial plans.

Good-better-best: Three-tier packaging model.

Base fee: Fixed charge before variable usage.

Minimum spend: Minimum amount payable within a period.

Overage: Usage exceeding included allowance.

Committed-use discount: Lower price in return for a usage commitment.

Volume discount: Reduced unit price at higher quantities.

Enterprise licence agreement: Broad agreement covering organisation-wide use.

Unlimited licence: Fixed fee for unrestricted use within defined boundaries.

Perpetual licence: One-time licence granting long-term usage rights.

Consumption pricing: Charges based on actual usage.

Credit-based pricing: Customers purchase credits consumed by different actions.

Freemium conversion: Percentage of free users becoming paid customers.

Price localisation: Adjusting prices for countries or markets.

Price realisation: Actual price achieved compared with list price.

List price: Published standard price.

Net price: Actual price after discounts and adjustments.

Pocket price: Amount remaining after all discounts, rebates and concessions.

Price waterfall: Analysis showing how list price becomes realised revenue.

Rebate: Retrospective payment or reduction based on conditions.

Price anchoring: Using a reference price to influence customer perception.

Decoy pricing: Introducing an option designed to make another option more attractive.

Van Westendorp analysis: Research method for acceptable price ranges.

## How to Actually Learn This

Do not try to memorise 500–800 terms alphabetically. Learn them through a single fictional AI company.

For every term, complete these seven fields:

| Field | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Definition | What does it mean in simple language? |
| Example | How would it appear in an AI company? |
| Owner | Which executive or department owns it? |
| Document | Where would I see it? |
| Metric | Which number measures it? |
| Risk | What happens when it is poorly managed? |
| CEO decision | What would I need to approve or challenge? |

For example:

| Term | AI-company example |
| --- | --- |
| Gross margin | AI subscription revenue minus model, infrastructure and support costs |
| RFP | A bank invites providers to propose an AI customer-service platform |
| DPA | Contract defining how the AI supplier processes customer data |
| NRR | Existing customers expand usage despite some cancellations |
| Model drift | Fraud model becomes less accurate as fraud patterns change |
| Cost per inference | Average model cost for processing one user request |
| Concentration risk | 55% of revenue depends on one banking customer |
| Source-code escrow | Customer receives protected access if the AI supplier fails |
| Step-in rights | Customer temporarily takes over critical operations after severe failure |
| Benefits realisation | Confirming that the AI implementation actually reduced handling time |

The priority for an AI-company CEO should be:

Tier 1: Strategy, finance, cash, sales, pricing and leadership
Tier 2: Procurement, contracts, customer success and operations
Tier 3: AI architecture, data, cybersecurity and governance
Tier 4: Tax, board governance, fundraising and M&A
Tier 5: ESG, international operations, supply chain and industry-specific terminology

A realistic executive target is not merely recognising the terms. It is being able to:

- explain each important term in plain English;

- ask the correct challenge question;

- identify the accountable executive;

- recognise the relevant commercial risk;

- interpret the associated metric;

- make or escalate the correct decision.
