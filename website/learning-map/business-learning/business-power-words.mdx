# Business Power Words

<ExecSummary>
Recommendation-led executive communication: structure, leadership verbs and commercial power phrases that turn technical work into decisions.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Before Partner, Director or client briefings.
- When writing proposals, SOWs or steering-committee updates.
- When practising the five-minute recommendation-led briefing from the workbook.
</WhenToUse>

### 1. The executive communication structure

Use this sequence when briefing Partners, Directors, clients or senior stakeholders:

Context → Insight → Business implication → Recommendation → Decision required

Example:

“Customer-service volumes have increased by 18%, while average handling time remains above target. Our analysis indicates that approximately 35% of enquiries are repetitive and suitable for AI-assisted resolution. This creates an opportunity to reduce cost-to-serve while improving response times. I recommend a controlled pilot covering three high-volume intents, with human escalation and measurable success criteria. Today, we need agreement on the pilot scope, business owner and risk appetite.”

This sounds stronger than describing every technical detail first.

## 2. High-impact leadership verbs

Use verbs that communicate ownership and movement:

Align, accelerate, assess, architect, clarify, coordinate, define, deliver, demonstrate, de-risk, enable, establish, evaluate, execute, facilitate, govern, influence, mobilise, operationalise, optimise, orchestrate, prioritise, rationalise, recommend, resolve, shape, simplify, standardise, strengthen, translate, validate.

Examples:

- “I will align the technical roadmap with the business priorities.”

- “We need to de-risk the integration before committing to scale.”

- “The objective is to operationalise the prototype, not simply demonstrate it.”

- “We should rationalise the number of models and platforms.”

- “I will orchestrate the work across architecture, security and delivery.”

- “We need to translate the technical capability into measurable business value.”

Avoid repeatedly using weak verbs such as help, look at, try, do, make, think.

## 3. Executive and strategic power words

#### Direction and strategy

Strategic intent, north star, target state, enterprise priority, strategic alignment, value proposition, differentiator, competitive advantage, market positioning, operating model, transformation agenda, capability roadmap, strategic dependency, decision horizon, investment thesis.

Useful phrases:

- “The strategic intent is to…”

- “This supports the client’s broader transformation agenda.”

- “The target state requires both technology and operating-model change.”

- “Our differentiator is not only the model; it is the governance, integration and delivery capability surrounding it.”

- “We should distinguish between a short-term tactical response and the long-term strategic platform.”

#### Prioritisation

Materiality, urgency, feasibility, desirability, viability, strategic fit, value-at-risk, business criticality, implementation complexity, time-to-value, dependency, sequencing.

Useful phrases:

- “We have prioritised this based on value, feasibility and risk.”

- “This is strategically important, but it is not the immediate critical path.”

- “The sequencing matters because identity, data access and governance are prerequisites.”

- “We should focus on the highest-value, lowest-regret actions first.”

## 4. Commercial and financial power words

As a Manager, connect technology decisions to economics.

Business case, commercial viability, return on investment, total cost of ownership, cost-to-serve, cost avoidance, revenue uplift, margin improvement, payback period, break-even point, unit economics, run-rate cost, implementation cost, consumption cost, value realisation, benefits tracking, commercial model, pricing model, funding envelope, investment case.

Useful phrases:

- “The solution is technically feasible, but we still need to validate commercial viability.”

- “The business case should include implementation cost, ongoing model consumption and operational support.”

- “The key question is whether the projected benefits justify the total cost of ownership.”

- “The largest value lever is cost avoidance rather than direct headcount reduction.”

- “We should establish a benefits baseline before implementation.”

- “The proposal needs clearer assumptions around volume, adoption and unit economics.”

- “This option provides faster time-to-value but a higher long-term run-rate.”

#### Commercial models

Fixed price, time and materials, outcome-based pricing, subscription, consumption-based pricing, managed service, licensing, gainshare, milestone-based payment, statement of work, change request, commercial assumption, acceptance criteria.

Example:

“A phased commercial model may be more appropriate: fixed price for discovery, time and materials for the prototype, followed by a managed-service model for production operations.”

## 5. Client discovery and consulting language

#### Discovery words

Business objective, pain point, root cause, current state, target state, user need, process constraint, control requirement, success measure, stakeholder expectation, decision criterion, non-functional requirement, assumption, dependency.

Powerful questions:

- “What business outcome are we ultimately trying to achieve?”

- “How is success measured today?”

- “What is preventing the current process from performing effectively?”

- “Which user groups experience the greatest friction?”

- “What decisions must the AI system support or automate?”

- “What level of autonomy is acceptable?”

- “Which risks would make the solution unacceptable?”

- “Who owns the business outcome after implementation?”

- “What evidence would give you confidence to proceed?”

- “Is the priority cost reduction, experience improvement, revenue generation or risk reduction?”

#### Clarification phrases

- “Let me confirm my understanding.”

- “The underlying issue appears to be…”

- “I would separate the symptom from the root cause.”

- “There are three distinct requirements within that request.”

- “Before we discuss technology, we should clarify the desired business outcome.”

- “That assumption needs to be validated with the process owner.”

- “Can we distinguish between a mandatory requirement and a preference?”

## 6. AI solution engineering power words

#### Architecture and engineering

Reference architecture, target architecture, solution pattern, integration boundary, orchestration layer, abstraction layer, service boundary, control plane, data plane, API contract, event-driven architecture, resilience, scalability, interoperability, extensibility, portability, observability, maintainability.

Useful phrases:

- “The architecture should remain model-agnostic where commercially practical.”

- “We need clear service boundaries to avoid tightly coupling the application to one provider.”

- “The integration pattern must support resilience, observability and controlled failure.”

- “This is a reusable enterprise capability rather than a single-use application.”

- “We should design for graceful degradation when the model or retrieval service is unavailable.”

- “The prototype proves technical feasibility; production readiness requires additional controls.”

#### AI-specific terminology

Grounding, retrieval-augmented generation, tool use, agent orchestration, model routing, prompt management, context engineering, deterministic workflow, probabilistic component, structured output, confidence threshold, fallback mechanism, human-in-the-loop, model evaluation, hallucination rate, task completion rate, guardrail, inference latency, token consumption, model drift.

Executive translation:

Instead of:

“We are using RAG with a vector database.”

Say:

“The assistant retrieves approved enterprise information before generating its answer, reducing reliance on the model’s internal knowledge and improving traceability.”

Instead of:

“We use agents.”

Say:

“The solution coordinates specialised AI components to complete multi-step tasks, while retaining controls over tool access, approvals and escalation.”

## 7. Security, risk and governance language

#### Risk terminology

Risk appetite, risk tolerance, control objective, inherent risk, residual risk, risk exposure, threat scenario, mitigating control, control effectiveness, risk owner, control owner, escalation threshold, audit evidence, assurance, accountability, traceability.

Useful phrases:

- “The question is not whether risk exists, but whether the residual risk is within the client’s appetite.”

- “We need a named risk owner and evidence that the control is operating effectively.”

- “This control reduces the likelihood, but not necessarily the impact.”

- “The current design creates an unacceptable concentration risk.”

- “The decision should be recorded with its assumptions and risk acceptance.”

- “We need to differentiate preventative, detective and corrective controls.”

#### Security power words

Zero trust, least privilege, defence in depth, secure by design, privacy by design, data minimisation, tenant isolation, encryption, secrets management, identity federation, privileged access, network segmentation, data loss prevention, vulnerability management, secure software development lifecycle.

Useful phrases:

- “Access should be role-based, least-privilege and auditable.”

- “Sensitive data should be minimised before it reaches the model.”

- “The architecture must assume that prompts and uploaded content are untrusted.”

- “We need defence in depth rather than relying on a single guardrail.”

- “Security requirements should be embedded in the design, not added before production.”

#### Responsible AI

Fairness, transparency, explainability, contestability, accountability, human oversight, proportionality, model limitations, acceptable use, prohibited use, impact assessment, model card, system card, evaluation evidence.

Example:

“The level of governance should be proportionate to the decision impact, data sensitivity and degree of autonomy.”

## 8. Delivery and programme-management power words

Deliverable, milestone, workstream, critical path, dependency, constraint, blocker, decision point, acceptance criteria, definition of done, delivery confidence, implementation readiness, resource capacity, scope boundary, change control, RAID log, mobilisation, transition, handover, operational readiness.

Useful phrases:

- “The programme remains achievable, but delivery confidence is dependent on timely data access.”

- “This is a dependency rather than a technical blocker.”

- “We need to protect the agreed scope while preserving flexibility in implementation.”

- “The acceptance criteria should be measurable and agreed before development begins.”

- “The issue requires a decision, not further analysis.”

- “We have identified the critical path and the decisions that could affect it.”

- “The workstream is progressing, but operational readiness remains below the required level.”

- “We should move from informal coordination to a clearly governed delivery structure.”

#### Status language

Instead of “Everything is going well”:

“Delivery remains on track against the agreed milestones, with two manageable risks relating to data access and security approval.”

Instead of “There are some issues”:

“There are three issues requiring attention: one delivery blocker, one commercial dependency and one governance decision.”

## 9. Stakeholder-management and influencing words

Stakeholder alignment, executive sponsorship, business ownership, decision authority, accountability, engagement, adoption, resistance, consensus, challenge, escalation, sponsorship, influence, credibility, trust.

Useful phrases:

- “We need active sponsorship, not only stakeholder awareness.”

- “The technology owner and business outcome owner should be clearly distinguished.”

- “There is alignment on the objective, but not yet on the implementation approach.”

- “I would like to surface a constructive challenge.”

- “We should resolve this at the appropriate decision-making level.”

- “The objective is not unanimous agreement; it is an informed and accountable decision.”

- “I recognise the concern. My recommendation remains unchanged because…”

- “Let us identify where we agree before addressing the remaining point of difference.”

## 10. Strong phrases for meetings

#### Opening

- “The purpose of today’s discussion is to…”

- “By the end of this session, we need agreement on…”

- “There are three decisions required today.”

- “I will briefly cover the context, recommendation and implications.”

- “The central question for this group is…”

#### Taking control

- “Let us bring the discussion back to the decision required.”

- “That is important, but it sits outside today’s scope.”

- “I suggest we separate the immediate decision from the longer-term design question.”

- “We are moving into implementation detail before agreeing the principle.”

- “Let us test that against the agreed success criteria.”

- “I am conscious of time, so I will summarise the position.”

#### Challenging professionally

- “I would challenge that assumption.”

- “I am not yet convinced the evidence supports that conclusion.”

- “There may be an alternative interpretation.”

- “That approach optimises for speed, but it introduces additional operational risk.”

- “I understand the rationale; however, the current proposal does not address…”

- “What would need to be true for that approach to succeed?”

- “What evidence are we using to support that estimate?”

#### Closing

- “To summarise, we have agreed…”

- “The outstanding decisions are…”

- “The named owner for this action is…”

- “The immediate next step is…”

- “I will circulate the decision, assumptions and actions.”

- “Unless there are objections, we will proceed on that basis.”

## 11. Strong phrases for emails and Teams messages

#### Requesting action

- “Please confirm your approval by…”

- “To maintain the delivery timeline, we require…”

- “Could you provide the following information to unblock the next stage?”

- “Please review the recommendation and highlight any material concerns.”

- “A decision is required on the following point.”

- “I would appreciate your direction on…”

#### Updating senior stakeholders

- “The key development since the last update is…”

- “Overall delivery remains on track.”

- “The primary risk requiring leadership attention is…”

- “No action is required from you at this stage.”

- “The matter has been contained, and corrective action is underway.”

- “We are escalating this because it may affect…”

#### Disagreeing diplomatically

- “I see the position differently.”

- “My concern with this approach is…”

- “I recommend an alternative for the following reasons.”

- “The proposal may need further validation before approval.”

- “I would not recommend proceeding without…”

- “This may solve the immediate problem while creating a larger downstream risk.”

## 12. Weak-to-powerful language replacements

| Weaker wording | Manager-level wording |
| --- | --- |
| “I think” | “My assessment is…” |
| “Maybe we should” | “I recommend that we…” |
| “We can try” | “We propose validating this through…” |
| “There is a problem” | “We have identified a material issue affecting…” |
| “It is expensive” | “The projected cost exceeds the current funding envelope.” |
| “It is risky” | “The residual risk is currently outside the agreed appetite.” |
| “We need more time” | “The current timeline does not allow sufficient validation.” |
| “This is better” | “This option performs more strongly against the agreed criteria.” |
| “They have not replied” | “Approval remains outstanding and is now affecting the critical path.” |
| “The system is slow” | “Latency exceeds the agreed performance threshold.” |
| “The AI made mistakes” | “Evaluation identified an unacceptable error rate in these scenarios.” |
| “We finished it” | “The deliverable has met the agreed acceptance criteria.” |
| “I don’t know” | “I need to validate that point before giving you a definitive answer.” |
| “That is not my job” | “That sits outside my ownership, but I will connect you with the accountable lead.” |
| “They caused the delay” | “The delay resulted from an unresolved cross-team dependency.” |

## 13. Power words for presentations and proposals

Use these as slide-title language:

- Executive recommendation

- Case for change

- Strategic rationale

- Current-state assessment

- Target-state vision

- Value opportunity

- Key design principles

- Options considered

- Recommended approach

- Commercial implications

- Risk and control considerations

- Delivery roadmap

- Operating-model impact

- Decision required

- Immediate next steps

- Path to production

- Value-realisation framework

- Scale and adoption plan

- Assumptions requiring validation

- Critical success factors

Avoid slide titles such as Overview, Information, Details, Some Considerations. A strong slide title should communicate the conclusion:

Weak:

“Model Options”

Strong:

“A multi-model strategy provides resilience but increases governance complexity”

## 14. The most useful consulting sentence patterns

#### Recommendation

“Based on the evidence available, I recommend [action] because it provides [benefit], while keeping [risk] within acceptable limits.”

#### Options

“We considered three options. Option A optimises for speed, Option B for control and Option C for long-term scalability.”

#### Risk

“The principal risk is [risk]. We can reduce the likelihood through [control], but the remaining exposure will require acceptance by [owner].”

#### Value

“The value case is driven by [cost reduction/revenue/risk avoidance], subject to the assumptions on [volume, adoption and performance].”

#### Escalation

“I am escalating this because the issue now affects [timeline, cost, scope or risk] and cannot be resolved within the existing workstream authority.”

#### Decision

“The decision required is whether to [action]. A decision by [date] is necessary to protect [business outcome or milestone].”

## 15. Your core PwC Manager vocabulary

These are the words I would prioritise mastering first:

Alignment, assurance, accountability, business outcome, commercial viability, control effectiveness, critical path, decision authority, dependency, differentiation, feasibility, governance, implementation readiness, materiality, operating model, operationalise, prioritisation, recommendation, residual risk, scalability, strategic fit, target state, time-to-value, traceability, value realisation.

A strong PwC Manager sentence often combines four of them:

“My recommendation is to prioritise the use cases with the strongest strategic fit and shortest time-to-value, while establishing the governance and traceability required for responsible scaling.”

The strongest communication principle is:

Be concise on the detail, precise on the risk, clear on the value and explicit about the decision required.
