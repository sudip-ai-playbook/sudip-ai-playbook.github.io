# Domain Knowledge Gathering

<ExecSummary>
The Smart Industry Learning System: Economics → Value chain → Stakeholders → Systems and data → Regulation → AI opportunities → Client proposition — run as a 10-hour sprint per industry.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Before starting a new industry engagement or proposal.
- When filling Industry Sprints, Battlecards and Domain Terms in the workbook.
- When you need domain fluency fast without drowning in passive reading.
</WhenToUse>

## The Smart Industry Learning System

Use the same seven-part structure for every industry:

Economics → Value chain → Stakeholders → Systems and data → Regulation → AI opportunities → Client proposition

You should be able to answer seven questions:

- How does this organisation make or receive money?

- What are its most important business processes?

- Which KPIs matter to its executives?

- What systems and data operate the business?

- What could go seriously wrong?

- Where can AI create measurable value?

- How would I explain the solution to the client?

When you can answer these, you have enough domain fluency to start shaping solutions.

## 1. Use a 10-hour sprint for each industry

You do not initially need weeks of study. Complete a focused 10-hour sprint, then deepen your knowledge when a real client opportunity appears.

### Hour 1: Understand the industry

Learn:

- main business models;

- customer types;

- revenue sources;

- largest costs;

- major competitors;

- current industry pressures.

Create a one-page Industry Executive Summary.

### Hours 2–3: Map the value chain

Identify the processes from the beginning of the business journey to the final outcome.

For a bank:

Customer acquisition → Onboarding → KYC → Account servicing → Payments/lending → Monitoring → Complaints → Retention

For a retailer:

Product planning → Sourcing → Warehousing → Merchandising → Marketing → Sales → Fulfilment → Returns

Mark each process as:

- customer-facing;

- operational;

- risk or control;

- finance or reporting;

- technology-enabled.

### Hour 4: Learn the stakeholders

For every domain, identify:

- economic buyer;

- business sponsor;

- technical buyer;

- risk approver;

- operational user;

- data owner;

- affected customer.

For example, a banking AI solution may involve:

- COO;

- Head of Operations;

- CIO;

- CDO;

- Chief Risk Officer;

- Compliance;

- Model Risk;

- Data Protection Officer;

- frontline employees.

### Hours 5–6: Learn systems and data

Do not try to memorise every product. Understand the categories:

- system of record;

- workflow system;

- customer platform;

- analytics platform;

- document repository;

- integration layer;

- security systems;

- industry-specific operational systems.

Then identify:

- structured data;

- unstructured data;

- real-time data;

- confidential data;

- personally identifiable data;

- regulated data.

### Hour 7: Learn regulation and critical risks

Focus on the regulations and risks that affect solution design.

Ask:

- Can AI make the decision?

- Is human review required?

- Must the decision be explainable?

- Can data leave the organisation?

- How long must evidence be retained?

- Could the outcome harm a customer, patient, employee or citizen?

- What audit evidence is required?

### Hours 8–9: Prioritise AI opportunities

Use a simple scoring model:

| Factor | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Business value | Does it increase revenue, reduce cost or reduce risk? |
| Feasibility | Is suitable data available? |
| Adoption | Will users incorporate it into their workflow? |
| Risk | Can the risks be controlled? |
| Scalability | Can the capability be reused? |
| Differentiation | Does it create competitive advantage? |

Score each factor from 1–5.

### Hour 10: Present a client case

Create a five-minute briefing containing:

- Client problem

- Current process

- Business impact

- Proposed AI capability

- Architecture overview

- Security and governance

- Implementation roadmap

- Expected value

- Key risks

- Decision required

This final exercise creates far more learning than passive reading.

## 2. What to learn first in each industry

### Financial Services

#### Learn first

- Banking, insurance and asset-management business models

- KYC, AML and financial crime

- Customer onboarding

- Lending and credit risk

- Payments

- Claims and underwriting

- Consumer Duty

- Operational resilience

- Model risk management

#### Essential processes

Banking

Lead → Application → Identity verification → Credit decision → Account opening → Servicing → Monitoring → Collections

Insurance

Quote → Underwrite → Bind → Policy servicing → Claim → Assessment → Settlement → Renewal

- cost-to-income ratio;

- net interest margin;

- default rate;

- fraud loss;

- straight-through processing rate;

- claims leakage;

- combined ratio;

- complaint-resolution time;

- customer retention.

## 3. Learn through one common AI use case

A very effective method is to study the same AI capability across different industries.

Use customer service as an example:

| Industry | Customer-service AI must understand |
| --- | --- |
| Banking | Accounts, payments, fraud, complaints and Consumer Duty |
| Insurance | Policies, claims, coverage and renewals |
| Telecoms | Plans, billing, provisioning and network incidents |
| Retail | Products, orders, delivery, returns and loyalty |
| Energy | Tariffs, meters, billing, outages and vulnerable customers |
| Government | Eligibility, applications, policy and appeals |
| Healthcare | Appointments, referrals, patient information and escalation |

The underlying architecture may be similar, but the following change:

- domain terminology;

- workflows;

- integrations;

- risk level;

- required evidence;

- escalation rules;

- success metrics.

This teaches you to distinguish reusable AI capability from industry-specific solution design.

## 4. Use the 30–20–10 learning method

For each industry, create:

### 30 domain terms

Examples for banking:

- net interest margin;

- loan-to-value;

- expected credit loss;

- KYC;

- AML;

- sanctions;

- arrears;

- collections;

- capital adequacy;

- operational resilience.

### 20 executive questions

Examples:

- What is driving the current cost-to-serve?

- Which process creates the highest customer friction?

- Where do cases require manual review?

- Which decisions require regulatory explanation?

- What evidence must be retained?

- Which legacy systems constrain automation?

- How will benefits be measured?

### 10 AI use cases

For every use case capture:

- problem;

- user;

- workflow;

- data;

- AI capability;

- integration;

- controls;

- KPI;

- value;

- implementation complexity.

That creates a complete first-level domain foundation.

## 5. Use active learning instead of passive reading

Avoid spending hours highlighting reports. Use this cycle:

### Step 1: Read

Spend no more than 30–45 minutes on one topic.

### Step 2: Close the source

Without looking, write:

- five things you learned;

- three terms you need to remember;

- two risks;

- one AI opportunity.

### Step 3: Explain it

Explain the industry aloud as though speaking to:

- a CEO;

- an operations leader;

- a solution architect;

- a risk officer.

Each audience requires different language.

### Step 4: Apply it

Design one AI solution or improve an existing process.

### Step 5: Retrieve later

Review after:

- one day;

- one week;

- one month.

This will produce stronger retention than repeatedly rereading material.

## 6. Create a one-page Industry Battlecard

Your battlecard should contain:

### Industry overview

- business models;

- customers;

- revenue;

- major costs;

- market pressures.

### Executive priorities

- growth;

- cost;

- customer experience;

- resilience;

- compliance;

- innovation.

### Value chain

A single end-to-end process diagram.

### KPIs

The ten most important metrics.

### Technology landscape

- core systems;

- major data sources;

- integration challenges;

- cloud and legacy considerations.

### Regulation

The principal regulator, laws and control expectations.

### AI opportunity map

- quick wins;

- strategic opportunities;

- high-risk opportunities;

- opportunities to avoid initially.

### Client questions

Ten discovery questions.

This should be the first document you open before an industry-related client discussion.

## 7. Create three levels of knowledge

Do not aim for the same depth across every sector.

### Level 1: Conversational fluency

You can:

- explain the business model;

- use basic terminology;

- identify stakeholders;

- discuss common AI opportunities.

Target: every PwC industry.

### Level 2: Solution fluency

You can:

- map processes;

- understand data and systems;

- discuss architecture;

- identify regulations;

- build a business case.

Target: four industries.

### Level 3: Advisory fluency

You can:

- challenge the client;

- compare operating models;

- understand regulatory nuances;

- quantify value;

- shape transformation programmes.

Target: two or three industries.

For you, I recommend:

- Level 3: Financial Services and TMT

- Level 2: Government, Healthcare, Consumer Markets and Energy

- Level 1: Manufacturing, Private Equity and remaining subsectors

## 8. Your 12-week accelerated schedule

### Weeks 1–2: Financial Services

Complete banking and insurance battlecards and design one regulated AI solution.

### Weeks 3–4: TMT

Study SaaS and telecommunications economics and create a telecom AI platform proposal.

### Weeks 5–6: Government and Healthcare

Study public-service case management, healthcare journeys and human-accountability requirements.

### Weeks 7–8: Consumer Markets

Study retail economics, demand planning and customer journeys.

### Weeks 9–10: Energy and Manufacturing

Study asset-heavy operations, predictive maintenance, IoT and operational technology.

### Week 11: Private Equity

Learn deals, due diligence, investment returns and portfolio value creation.

### Week 12: Executive simulation

Prepare three client presentations:

- Banking onboarding AI

- Telecom customer-service AI

- Government caseworker copilot

For each, present:

- business case;

- proposed architecture;

- governance model;

- implementation roadmap;

- value;

- risks.

## 9. How to know you have learned an industry

You are ready when you can complete these tasks without notes:

- explain the industry in three minutes;

- draw its value chain;

- identify ten domain terms;

- name its major executive KPIs;

- identify the core systems and datasets;

- explain three regulatory risks;

- propose five AI use cases;

- ask ten strong discovery questions;

- present one credible AI solution;

- explain why the client should invest now.

The best next step is to build your first complete Financial Services AI Solution Engineering Learning Pack, using this method as the template for all subsequent industries.
