# Competition and monopoly

<ExecSummary>
A valuable company creates X dollars of value and captures Y% of X — X and Y are independent. Aim for creative monopoly; avoid perfect competition. Start in tiny markets and expand concentrically. Prefer being last mover over first mover.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Choosing beachhead markets.
- Evaluating whether an industry can ever capture value.
- Resisting “huge TAM on day one” pitch theatre.
</WhenToUse>

## Value creation vs capture

Airlines: huge X, tiny Y (≈0 cumulative US profits over a century). Google search: smaller revenue than airlines historically, far more valuable. Perfect competition is easy to model and praised politically — miserable if you are in it.

Binary frame (useful exaggeration): **perfect competition** vs **monopoly**. Little in between. Everyone lies: monopolists claim fierce competition (regulation); non-monopolists claim unique niches (fundraising).

## Market storytelling lies

| Actor | Lie |
| --- | --- |
| Non-monopoly | “We’re the only British restaurant in Palo Alto” (fake intersection) |
| Monopoly | “We’re in advertising / tech broadly” (fake huge union) |

Startups with buzzword intersections (“sharing mobile social”) often describe the nothing of nowhere.

## How to build monopoly

1. **Start small** — Amazon books → commerce; eBay Pez → auctions; PayPal eBay power sellers; Facebook Harvard. Business schools undervalue tiny starts.
2. **Avoid giant markets day one** — cleantech “trillion-dollar energy” decks meant minnows in oceans of competitors.
3. Characteristics: proprietary tech (~10× better), network effects, economies of scale, brand (invest carefully).
4. Software: near-zero marginal cost → fast scale with the market.

## Last mover

Most DCF value is decades out. Growth is measurable; **durability** is undervalued. Ask why you are still the leader in 10–20 years. Capablanca: study the endgame.

## History lesson

Science and many tech waves: Y ≈ 0% for inventors. Railroads, Wright brothers, textile mills — value competed away. Rare captures: vertically integrated complex monopolies (Ford, Standard Oil; Tesla/SpaceX integration) and software network businesses.

## Psychology

Humans are mimetic (ape = primate *and* imitate). Competition as validation is often insanity (20k actors / year → ~20 make it). Kissinger on academia: ferocious battles, tiny stakes. Prefer the vast gate nobody is rushing.

Lean startup / endless iteration: Thiel is skeptical — great companies often make a quantum leap, not survey-driven sameness. Risk of a meaningless life can exceed “safe” career risk.

<KeyTakeaways>

- Capture matters as much as creation.
- Tiny beachhead → expand; last mover durability.
- Escaping competition is the point of strategy.

</KeyTakeaways>
