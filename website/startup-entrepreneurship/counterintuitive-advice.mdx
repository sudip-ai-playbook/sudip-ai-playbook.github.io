# Counterintuitive advice

<ExecSummary>
Startup instincts often lead you astray (like leaning back on skis). Trust instincts about people; distrust instincts that make you “play house.” You need expertise in users, not expertise in startups. In college: learn and meet co-founders — do not force a company.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- When you catch yourself optimising fundraising theatre.
- When choosing college / career prep for a future founding bet.
</WhenToUse>

## The list (Paul Graham)

### 1. Instincts mislead

Counterintuitive advice feels wrong — founders ignore partners, then wish they had listened. Pause before “obvious” moves.

Exception: **people**. Trust misgivings about impressive but distasteful characters. Pick collaborators as you would friends; wait until interests diverge to see the truth.

### 2. Expertise in users, not startups

Zuckerberg succeeded despite startup incompetence (Florida LLC) because he understood users. Mechanics of angel rounds matter far less than making something people want.

Learning fundraising theatre in detail can be **dangerous** — it encourages going through the motions.

### 3. Gaming the system stops working

School trains extracurriculars and exam tricks. Startups call that **playing house**: plausible idea → raise → Soma office → hire friends → realise you never made something people want.

“How do I …?” usually answers “Just ….” Translate “growth hacks” to nonsense until you have product love. Users are sharks: meat or no meat.

You can fool investors briefly; you cannot fool retention graphs forever — and you are wasting your own equity years.

### 4. All-consuming

Success does not get easier; worry volume stays high. Like having kids: irrevocable button. Universities push entrepreneurship while warning against early parenthood — inconsistent.

### 5. You cannot tell if you are up to it

Prior life predicts little. Swagger ≠ toughness. If terrified, maybe skip; if unsure, try — **later**, not as a student by default.

### 6. Ideas come from living, not brainstorming

Conscious “startup idea” sessions produce bad-but-plausible ideas. Become the kind of mind that has ideas unconsciously:

1. Learn about things that matter  
2. Work on interesting problems  
3. With people you like and respect  

Best companies began as side projects. Live at the leading edge of some technology (“live in the future”). Ultimate advice: **just learn**.

Business school for entrepreneurship? Mostly no — designed for large-company management. Domain expertise and building beat MBA theatre.

<KeyTakeaways>

- Users over startup cosplay.
- College: learn + co-founders; rarely incorporate.
- Interesting problems and good people beat idea contests.

</KeyTakeaways>
