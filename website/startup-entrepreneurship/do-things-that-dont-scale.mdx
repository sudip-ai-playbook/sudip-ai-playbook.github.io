# Do things that don't scale

<ExecSummary>
Unscalable tactics are a startup advantage: personal recruiting, founder-delivered service, duct-tape ops. Use them to learn and to earn champions — give them up only when ripped away, not on a funding milestone.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Zero to first users.
- Finding product–market fit.
- Turning early customers into advocates.
</WhenToUse>

## Test → launch → hack

**DoorDash:** Interview merchants → afternoon landing page (PDF menus + phone) → founders deliver Pad Thai → Google Docs + Square + Find My Friends. Demand showed up despite ugly UX. Later automate dispatch.

**Teespring:** Days of free design for tiny non-profit orders — looked terrible from outside; boulder uphill then rolls. Delight with support (founders on tickets); make wrongs right; speed over clean architecture for next order of magnitude only.

## Three jobs for unscalable work

1. **Find first users** — no silver-bullet PPC; founders’ personal time (emails, calls, conferences, street fairs, water bottles at fairs). Prefer paid validation over free forever.
2. **Turn users into champions** — exceptional experiences; founders on support; proactive outreach to churned users; own social reputation.
3. **Find product–market fit** — duplicate codebases if needed; crash nightly if necessary; worry about 10→100 not 10→1,000,000.

## Manual learning

Doing the work (cleaning, delivering, photographing Airbnb homes) teaches algorithms and product. Email every new customer personally. Capacity constraints (ice cream vs delivery spike) motivate scaling.

## When to stop

As long as net-positive, keep doing unscalable things. Smaller competitors can still out-love you if you abandon the advantage too early.

<KeyTakeaways>

- Launch experiments fast; founders are the ops stack.
- Handhold early users; optimise for learning speed.
- Scale systems after demand is undeniable.

</KeyTakeaways>
