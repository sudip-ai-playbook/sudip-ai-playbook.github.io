# Execution

<ExecSummary>
Founders sign up for a years-long grind of execution, not magazine covers. Culture is modeled after founders. Focus on two or three priorities; intensity and bias to action separate winners; never lose growth momentum.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Weekly planning for an early team.
- Diagnosing “working hard but not winning.”
- Recovering after a momentum dip.
</WhenToUse>

## CEO jobs (early)

1. Set the vision  
2. Raise money  
3. Evangelise (recruits, employees, partners, press, customers)  
4. Hire and manage the team  
5. **Set the execution bar** — only the CEO can do this

Execution = figure out what to do + get it done. Focus and intensity are how you get it done.

## Focus

Ask: what are we spending time and money on? That reveals priorities.

- ~100 things compete daily; pick **2–3**, ignore/defer/delegate the rest.
- Law firms, conferences, advisors often do not matter early.
- Say no ~97 times out of 100.
- Company-wide goals everyone can recite (ship date, growth rate, key hires, deal).
- Repeat goals obsessively (walls, 1:1s, all-hands).
- Co-location aids focus; communication breakdowns scramble small teams.

Never accept “we’re not focused on growth right now” for long. PR without results is a common distraction.

## Intensity

Startups work at a fairly intense level. Extreme focus + dedication. Tiny differences matter (viral coefficient 0.99 vs 1.01). Outwork competitors a little on the *right* things.

Move fast **and** obsess over quality (Facebook: move fast and break things *while* caring about quality). Bias to action: best YC predictor is teams that have **done new things** every time you talk to them. Incremental chunks compound; disappearing for a year usually fails.

Show up — literally get on planes in marginal situations when a critical deal is at stake.

## Momentum

Prime directive: keep the company winning. Growth for software; ship dates for hardware.

If momentum sags: skip vision speeches; chase **small wins** (sales fix everything). Disagreement rises when losing — ask users and do what they say. Facebook’s growth group (2008) turned small product fixes into prestige and recovery.

Operating rhythm: regular shipping, weekly metrics review. Board as forcing function for milestones more than strategy genius.

Competitors’ press: ignore until they ship a real product. “Press releases are easier than code.” Ford: fear the competitor who never bothers about you and improves their business constantly.

<KeyTakeaways>

- Founders set the bar by personal example.
- Few priorities, high intensity, continuous shipping.
- Protect growth metrics; recover with small wins, not speeches.

</KeyTakeaways>
