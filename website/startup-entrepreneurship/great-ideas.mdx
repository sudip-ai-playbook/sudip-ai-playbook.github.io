# Great ideas

<ExecSummary>
Ideas matter. Great execution on a terrible idea still fails. Define “idea” broadly: market size and growth, strategy, defensibility. Prefer unpopular-but-right kernels in small, fast-growing markets with a clear “why now.”
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Evaluating whether to build V1.
- Choosing among several plausible ideas.
- Writing the first sentence of a pitch.
</WhenToUse>

## What “idea” includes

Not just the product concept — also:

- Market size **and** growth over ~10 years
- Growth and defensibility strategy
- Whether you can get a monopoly in a **small** beachhead, then expand

Plans are worthless; planning is valuable. Long-term thinking is scarce and therefore an advantage.

## Pendulum mistakes

| Fashionable claim | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Ideas don't matter; just ship and pivot | Execution ≫ idea (~10× importance, ~100× harder) — but a bad idea stays bad |
| More pivots = better | Successful pivots usually into something founders already wanted; random pivots rarely become huge |
| V1 must sound massive | V1 should own a small specific market |

Google (13th search engine), Facebook (college-only), Airbnb (strangers’ couches) all sounded terrible — and therefore under-competed.

## Mission and compulsion

- Work on the idea you think about when you are *not* trying to work.
- Wait until something feels compulsory; many founders wish they had waited.
- Hard important missions attract help; derivative companies do not.

Good startups usually take **~10 years**. “Two years then I’ll do what I love” almost never works.

## Markets

Prefer **small + rapidly growing** over large + stagnant. Investors obsess over today’s TAM; founders should obsess over growth rate and whether the market will top out.

- You cannot create a market that does not want to exist.
- Students often have better intuition for emerging markets than older investors.
- Sequoia’s question: **Why now?** Why not two years ago? Why too late in two years?

Build something you need when possible. Otherwise sit in the customer’s office and talk to them constantly.

## Shape of a good idea

- Explainable in **one sentence**
- Either sharply different on one axis (Google = search that works) or truly new (SpaceX) — not “X with prettier design” or “Y for red-wine drinkers”
- Sounds crazy, is right; few competitors
- Path: monopoly in niche → concentric expansion (see [Competition and monopoly](./competition-and-monopoly))

Conviction vs crazy is a fine line. Expect most people to hate a great idea — that is a feature.

## Student advantages

1. Intuition for young markets  
2. Best environment to meet **co-founders** (more important than any particular startup)

Think market and customer demand first — the most common YC application failure.

<DeliverableBox>
Expected outputs:

- One-sentence problem + solution
- Why now (≤5 bullets)
- Beachhead market + expansion sketch
- Defensibility notes (network, tech 10×, brand, scale)
</DeliverableBox>

<KeyTakeaways>

- Kernel first; ambition expands later.
- Unpopular but right; easy to explain; hard to replicate.
- Market growth and “why now” beat impressive-sounding TAM slides.

</KeyTakeaways>
