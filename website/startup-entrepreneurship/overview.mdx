# Startup and Entrepreneurship

This section turns **How to Start a Startup** (Stanford CS183B / Y Combinator) into a usable playbook: what to do early, what to ignore, and how the advice changes after product–market fit.

<ExecSummary>
Hyper-growth startups win on **idea × product × team × execution × luck**. You control the first four. Passion for a problem must come before “I want to start a company.” Everything else in this section is how to raise those four levers without gaming the system.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- You are exploring whether to start a company, join an early team, or advise founders.
- You need a shared language for idea quality, product love, hiring, growth and culture.
- You want tactical checklists for fundraising, sales, legal mechanics and post-PMF scaling.
</WhenToUse>

> **Source note:** Synthesised from [CS183B: How to Start a Startup](https://startupclass.samaltman.com/) (Sam Altman and guest lectures). Practitioner summary, not a transcript. Prefer the original talks and essays (especially Paul Graham) when you go deep.

## The four controllable areas

| Area | Core question | Start here |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Idea** | Mission, market timing, small monopoly beachhead | [Great ideas](./great-ideas) |
| **Product** | Do a small number of users *love* it? | [Great products](./great-products) |
| **Team** | Relentlessly resourceful co-founders; hire late and slow | [Team and hiring](./team-and-hiring) |
| **Execution** | Focus, intensity, momentum, decisive action | [Execution](./execution) |

Outcome ≈ idea × product × team × execution × **luck** (luck is a huge random factor). Excel at the four you control.

## Hard warnings (up front)

- Advice here targets **hyper-growth** companies. Much of it fails inside large orgs or lifestyle businesses.
- Never start a startup *for the sake of starting*. Passion for the problem first.
- Until users love the product, almost everything else (PR, partnerships, growth hacks) is noise.

<KeyTakeaways>

- Control idea, product, team and execution; accept luck as a multiplier.
- Prefer a small market you can monopolise and expand over a large competitive one.
- Love from few users beats lukewarm liking from many.
- Culture and process matter after PMF — not as a substitute for shipping.

</KeyTakeaways>
