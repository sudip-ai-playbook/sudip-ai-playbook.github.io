# Why start a startup

<ExecSummary>
Start only if you feel compelled by a problem and believe a company is the best way to solve it. Glamour, “being the boss,” schedule freedom and “bigger equity slice” are usually the wrong primary reasons.
</ExecSummary>

<WhenToUse>
- Before incorporating or quitting a job for a founding bet.
- When mentoring students or employees who romanticise entrepreneurship.
</WhenToUse>

## The right reason

**You cannot not do it** — in two senses:

1. **Passion:** You will keep working on it anyway (nights at Facebook on Asana’s precursor; Airbnb from unpaid rent + spare space).
2. **Fit:** The world needs the thing, and you are unusually suited to build it. If not, you may out-compete a better-suited team into a worse outcome for everyone.

Mission-oriented companies recruit better, endure the decade-long grind, and attract outside help. Derivative “X for Y” copies rarely excite teams enough to win.

## Reasons that mislead

| Temptation | Reality |
| --- | --- |
| **Glamour** | Mostly desk work, support, sales, hard engineering — not parties and “brilliant insight” montages |
| **Be the boss** | You disappoint someone every day; employee crises override your grand plans |
| **Flexibility** | Always on-call; team mirrors your energy; four-day weeks rarely survive past a tiny niche business |
| **Impact / money** | Joining a rocket ship (employee ~100 at Facebook/Dropbox-era outcomes) can beat a mediocre $100M “Uber for petsitting” |

Hollywood and press compress years of grind into insight + champagne. Founder depression and stress are real; managing your own psychology is a core CEO job (Ben Horowitz).

## Opportunity cost

A successful startup can take a decade. Students: **do not start in college by default** — explore, meet co-founders, learn deeply (Paul Graham). If a side project takes over your life, you will know. Prefer serendipity and domain expertise over vocational “entrepreneurship theatre.”

## Decision checklist

- [ ] I can name the problem in one sentence and why *I* must solve it
- [ ] Starting a company is better than joining / open-sourcing / lobbying
- [ ] I accept stress, commitment (~5–10 years if it works) and weak work–life balance
- [ ] I am not starting mainly for status, schedule or “being CEO”

<KeyTakeaways>

- Specific passion first; startup second.
- Benchmark equity upside against joining breakout companies.
- Reluctance + obsession (Asana) beats “I want to be a founder.”

</KeyTakeaways>
